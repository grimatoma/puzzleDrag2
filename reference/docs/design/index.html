What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and you outfit a caravan to roll out and found new towns across the map, opening and developing several at your own pace. Each town stacks a new board on the last and grows bigger and harder, but the heart never changes: drag, harvest, grow, complete, move on.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm, build a caravan, and head out across the map to found new towns — a river valley, a volcano's cooled terraces, a sea-cliff harbour, a rat-plagued meadow, a deep crystal delve — until you raise the Golden Capital and wake the kingdom.

Design pillars

One compounding loop

Every system feeds a single flywheel: play the board → harvest → spend on growth → the board yields more. Depth comes from stacking on that loop, never from a second, parallel game.

Cozy early, ramping late

The first sites are forgiving teachers. Hazards, bosses and scarcity arrive only once the player has the tools to meet them. Effort-gated, never time-gated — you are never stuck waiting on a clock.

Choose your build

Plots are scarce, so which buildings, workers and tools you raise is a real decision. You can demolish to re-spec. The puzzle math stays fixed; you change what the board hands you.

A world that grows

The map is the progression bar. Each settlement visibly expands from outpost to city in place, and the frontier of foundable towns unfurls outward as you go.

The core loop

Everything is one compounding loop: play the board to harvest; spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; the next round is bigger. Every system in this set either feeds that flywheel or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — your first chain to your last monument.

Boards build on each other

The backbone — and what keeps it from being "the same board over and over": a new board type is always fed by the boards before it. The farm makes food; food buys mine expeditions; farm food (bait) plus mine metal (tackle) fund the fishery. So every town with a mine also has a farm, and every fishery town has a farm and a mine. The one deliberate exception is the deep mine town (Glimmerdeep) — a mine with no farm, where provisioning under scarcity is the whole point.

The puzzles stack, and earlier boards never idle — later boards keep buying from them. This feed-dependency is also the game's main soft gate.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board: a small budget of turns (~10), each chain spends one, whatever you harvest banks into that town's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Each town is a long project, not a handful of sessions — finishing one takes real effort. But it is effort-gated, not time-gated: you are never stuck waiting on a clock. The time between sessions hands you free tools (timed depots, daily, civic) to go faster — or, if you'd rather not wait, you craft those tools now by spending extra materials.

The long arc is a map of settlements — a required spine of seven that the finale needs, plus optional frontier towns you can take on for content and cross-zone bonuses. You found each by caravan, build it up, and beat its signature task — but you need not finish one before opening the next: several towns can be in progress at once and you switch between them freely. Difficulty is cozy early, ramping late: the rising founding cost plus the map's outward-expanding reach keep the harder, farther sites for later. Workers, coins, tools and Villagers travel with you; each town's materials are its own. See Progression for the full map and gate model.

What is — and isn't — in scope

A deliberate line so the design and the build stay honest. Per the current direction we fully spec the whole arc, but only the early towns are tuning-locked; the later ones are specced-but-not-yet-balanced.

In scope — the spec

  • One farm board, one mine board, one fish board — stacked, never parallel mini-games.
  • A required 7-town spine (farm → +mine → +fish → mine-only → capital) plus 5 optional frontier towns.
  • Per-town tier ladders that raise plots and unlock buildings; non-linear founding with light soft gates.
  • Three currencies (materials, coins, Villagers), crafting, workers, tools, expeditions, hazards & bosses, boons.
  • The always-on meta wrap: story, quests, daily, civic, milestones; a free-to-play money model.
  • Four-season cycle as the signature visual; a narrative capstone finale (build the Monument).

Out of scope (for now)

  • Multiplayer / social of any kind. Single-player, offline-friendly.
  • A second core game bolted on (PvP arena, base-defense, gacha summon). Depth stacks on the one loop.
  • Per-town economies that don't feed the loop — every board must pay into growth.
  • Deep balance of the late arc. Towns 4–7 + frontier are specced; their numbers are provisional until the cross-zone pacing sim validates them.
  • Permanent global power creep. Cross-zone exports are bounded, named bonuses — not a stat-stacking meta-tree.
Tuning-locked vs specced. "Fully spec the whole arc" means every settlement has a design (environment, boards, hazard, boss, win task, tier ladder). It does not mean every number is final. Early towns are tuning-locked; later towns carry provisional numbers flagged in Economy and Build status.
