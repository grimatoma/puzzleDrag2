From a Cold Hearth to the Old Capital

one full playthrough — the board, the town, and the whole economy — told as a story

This is a single, start-to-finish walk through the game — not a manual and not a wiki, just the shape of one complete run: what you see, what you do, how the tiles you unlock change the way the board plays, what you actually build, where every coin and every resource comes from and where it goes, and how a dead little settlement grows back into a living kingdom. Read it top to bottom the way you'd play it.

1 The opening

A cold hearth

You arrive at Hearthwood Vale — your home — and it is quiet and cold. The hearth at its center has gone out. An old caretaker, Wren, presses a pair of iron tongs into your hands, asks you to name the place, and tells you the truth of it plainly: light the hearth, and the Vale begins to wake. Everything else follows from that first warmth.

You name your settlement. The board slides up in front of you. You make your first drag — and the game has begun.

2 How the board plays

Drag, chain, collect

The board is a small grid of living tiles — tufts of grass, stalks of grain, trees, birds. You play by pressing on a tile and dragging across its neighbors: every tile of the same kind that you touch joins a chain behind your finger, snaking in any of the eight directions. Drag through three or more and the chain lights up; let go, and it collects.

Collecting does three things at once. The tiles you gathered turn into a resource dropped into your stores — grass becomes hay, grain becomes flour, trees become planks, birds become eggs, fruit becomes pie — and the harvest also pays you a few coins on the spot. If the chain was long enough to cross a threshold, a higher upgrade tile blooms where you finished, so good chains seed better boards. And any partner tiles sitting right beside your chain get swept up too — a cross-collect bonus (grass pulls in grain, fruit pulls in trees, herds pull in vegetables) that rewards reading the whole board, not just one line. Then everything above slides down to fill the gaps and fresh tiles drop in from the top.

The rhythm of a session

Each visit to a board is a handful of turns — roughly ten. As your turns burn down, the seasons drift across the board behind your play: spring greens into summer, ambers into autumn, then settles toward winter, and each season quietly reweights what the board hands you. It's the visual heartbeat of a run. When the turns are gone, a harvest summary tallies what you made and you head back to town. What you're really chasing the whole time: longer chains, clever cross-collect adjacencies, and not wasting turns.

3 The living deck

Unlocking tiles, and the gifts they carry

You don't play the same board forever. The set of tiles that can appear is a collection you grow, and most of the game's depth lives here. The plain starters — grass, wheat, an apple tree, a pheasant, a pig — are with you from the first drag. Everything past them you have to earn, and there are five different ways a new tile enters your world:

Five ways to unlock a tile

  • By chaining — collect enough of a kind and a relative shows up. Chain twenty grass over time and Meadow Grass joins the pool; chain eighteen apples and the Golden Apple appears.
  • By research — pour research points into a kind to coax out a variant (fifty into grass unlocks Spiky Grass; twenty into eggs unlocks the Turkey).
  • By buying it — pay coins outright. A Clover is 200, a Dodo 250, a Melon 500 — and the joke-rare Pig in Disguise a flat 100,000.
  • By owning a building — some tiles only show once you've raised the building that justifies them (the Kitchen brings broccoli to the fields).
  • By daily login — a few are calendar rewards for showing up, like the Triceratops that lands on day 30.

And unlocking a tile isn't just a new picture — most of them carry an ability that changes how the board plays. This is the part the word "deck" really earns: you're tuning the machine.

What the new tiles actually do

Some hand you free moves each season just for being on the board — a clover or a palm is worth two, a melon a generous five — so a turn-poor session loosens up. Some seed more of their own kind: Meadow Grass adds a grass slot to the spawn pool, Spiky Grass adds two, thickening the board with the thing you most want to chain. Some pay extra coins — a Golden Apple tips five coins every harvest; the rare Golden Coin tile pays twenty per tile you collect with it. And some bend the turn economy: a humble Pig refunds a free move any time you chain six or more. Building your collection is really building a strategy.

A long chain doesn't just pay out — where you let go, a tile of the next tier blooms in place, so good play literally upgrades the board under you: grass climbs toward Meadow then Spiky, an apple toward a Pear then a Golden Apple, a pig toward a Hog, a Boar, a Warthog. How long a chain has to be to trigger that bloom depends on the family — grain comes easy at five, most farm tiles want six, fruit and flowers and mounts hold out for seven to ten.

A few tiles aren't crops at all

Out in the harder lands, special tiles show up on a timer and ask to be caught. Deep in a mine a Mysterious Ore surfaces once a session with a five-turn fuse — chain it together with a couple of dirt tiles before it decays and it pays a Rune, the rare currency that powers the late-game Portal. On the coast a Giant Pearl does the same, asking to be chained with the fish around it. Miss the window and they slump back into ordinary tiles. They're the board's little heists.

4 Lighting the hearth

From a tile, to a table, to a town that grows

Your first goal is small and warm: gather enough hay to light the hearth. You harvest your first tile, then twenty bundles of hay, and the fire catches — Mira arrives to keep you company, and soon Old Tomas too. Now the loop opens up. Neighbors post orders — bring me bread, bring me soup — and filling them pays coins (more the closer your bond with that neighbor). But a raw tile rarely satisfies an order on its own; most of what the town wants has to be made.

That's the second half of the economy: the board grows raw resources, and your crafting stations turn them into finished goods. The chain from dirt to dinner is the spine of every farm run:

🌾 Mill + Bakery: wheat → flour, then flour + eggs → bread (and honey rolls, harvest pies, a festival loaf)
🫙 Larder: blackberries → jam, then jam + eggs → preserves; fish + milk + soup → chowder
⚒️ Forge: iron + coke → hinges and lanterns; gold bars → rings and gem-crowns
🍲 Kitchen / Smokehouse: flour → supplies; meat + coke → cured meat — the food that fuels expeditions

Finished goods are worth far more than the raw stuff they came from — that's what fills the lucrative orders and, later, what you sell at market for coins. So a farm run isn't just "make hay." It's: chain the fields, haul the raw goods home, run them through the right stations, and turn the output into coins and buildings.

Spend coins on hands that ease the work

Coins go furthest on workers. Some are paid to make a crop need a shorter chain — hire a grain hand and a run of wheat that used to demand six tiles pays off at five, so every drag does more. Others cut your crafting costs (a baker shaves a flour off every loaf) or simply tip extra coins per chain (a tax collector, a florist). Each hire stacks, up to about ten, so the benefit deepens the more you commit. Hiring costs a few coins and a villager — and villagers come from housing, so your population caps how many hands you can keep. It's one of the main ways an early grind softens into an easy, generous rhythm: you're quietly buying the work down.

Tools — single-use bursts you craft at the Workshop

Once you've built a Workshop, raw materials become tools, and tools are how you break a board open. A Bomb blows a 3×3 hole where you tap (your Powder Store quietly stamps out two every season). A Sickle reaps a whole row; an Auger bores a column; a Blast Charge clears a full cross. A Plough harvests every grass and grain tile at once; a Fruit Picker gathers all the fruit. When a board's gone stale, a Reshuffle Horn deals a fresh one and a Magic Seed simply hands you five more turns. Mines get their own kit — drills, stone hammers, water pumps to flood out lava — and the rarest, Portal-summoned tools (the Magic Wand that sweeps every tile of a type you tap, the Philosopher's Stone that turns stone to gold) bend the rules outright.

As you raise buildings, the town itself levels up beneath you — from a bare Outpost to a Hamlet, then a Village, then a full City. Each tier-up costs a basket of the goods you've been making, and each one widens the town: more plots to build on, and a longer list of buildings you're allowed to raise.

5 What you'd actually build

One farm town, Outpost to City

Here's the catch that gives a town its character: at every tier you unlock far more buildings than you have plots for. So a playthrough of Hearthwood Vale isn't a checklist — it's a sequence of real choices. This is roughly how one run fills in, and why:

Outpost3 plots · 5 unlocked
The Hearth is free and anchors the place, so it never costs a plot-choice. That leaves two real picks from Mill, Bakery, Granary and Inn. Take the Bakery (bread is your first money-maker) and the Granary — it pays for itself with +1 turn every farm session and +300 storage. The Mill and Inn wait.
Hamlet6 plots · +3 unlocked
Now Housing (your first villagers, which means your first workers), a Larder to bottle preserves for richer orders, and a Silo to keep your field layout between visits. The squeeze eases — but only for a moment.
Village12 plots · +9 unlocked
The pressure returns hard: nine new buildings, far more than the doubling of plots covers. Yield-boosters earn their keep here — a Sawmill (+1 plank per oak chain), an Apiary (+1 honey per pansy), a Stable (+1 horseshoe per horse). Add the Kitchen (it bakes the supplies that get you into mines), a second Housing, and a Chapel that simply pays +50 coins a season. Brewery, Watchtower and Apothecary are the ones you'll likely leave out.
City20 plots · +13 unlocked
Peak pressure: close to thirty buildings ever unlocked, twenty plots ever available. The keystones go in — Workshop (tools), Forge (metal goods, the high-coin crafts), Caravan Post (sell anything at market), the third Housing, an Observatory (gems upgrade a step sooner), and the Portal. You physically cannot fit a Clock Tower, a Lighthouse, a Powder Store and a Brewery on top of all that — so a City is a statement about what your town is for.
Buildings are bought with resources, not coins

Almost nothing on that ladder costs coins — buildings are paid in the goods you've been hauling and crafting. A Bakery is 8 planks, 6 hay and 4 eggs. A Granary is 12 bread, 6 stone blocks and 4 iron bars. A City-tier Forge wants 15 iron bars. So "building your town" is really a demand on your whole production chain — which is exactly why you mine and fish and bake: to feed the next plot. Later you can knock a building down and re-plan, but plots stay precious, and what you leave out matters as much as what you raise.

6 Coins, and the road out

How you pay your way to the next land

One quick clarification, because the two get confused: coins are the money — the single currency that follows you everywhere. Gold in this world is a metal you mine, a gold bar, that goes into the Forge for rings and gem-crowns. You don't spend gold bars to travel; you spend coins. So the real question — "how do I afford the next zone?" — is a question about coins, and coins come from everywhere you play:

Where coins come from

  • Harvesting chains — every collect pays coins (roughly tiles × their value); the steady drip that funds everything.
  • Filling orders — neighbor requests pay out, scaled up by how well you know that neighbor.
  • Daily quests & the almanac — small bounties and login rewards that climb from a few coins to a thousand.
  • The market — once you've a Caravan Post, sell any crafted good.
  • Standing income — a Chapel pays +50 a season; coin-tiles and beaten Keepers drop lump sums.

Where coins go

  • Founding a new town — the big one. Your second settlement costs 300 coins, and the price climbs ×1.7 each time after (≈510, then ≈867…).
  • Entering a board — every session has a toll: 50 coins for a farm or the first harbor, 100 for the mines and the deep marsh, 200 for the Black Forge.
  • Hiring workers — each hire costs coins, ramping as you stack them.
  • Decorations — vanity that buys town prestige, 60–400 coins each.

But coins alone don't open the map — there's a progress gate too. Founding is non-linear: you can settle any site you can reach and afford. Reaching the mountains, though, means your home has to have grown all the way to a City first; the deeper Lanternlit Caves, the Coast, and the marsh wait until your first quarry hits its early tiers; the Black Forge waits until that quarry is a real Mining Camp. So the road out is paced by two things at once — the coins in your purse and how far you've already come. You never have to finish one town before starting the next; you can have several in progress and switch between them freely, leaving a place that isn't grabbing you and coming back later.

7 The economy at a glance

Every faucet, every drain

Strip away the story and the whole game is one balance sheet: things that generate resources, and things that consume them. A healthy run keeps the left side ahead of the right. Here is the entire ledger in one place.

Generators — where resources are made

  • The board — the source of everything. Each tile family yields one raw good: grass→hay, grain→flour, trees→planks, birds→eggs, herds→meat, cattle→milk, mounts→horseshoes, flowers→honey, fruit→pie, veg→soup; mines give blocks, iron/copper/silver/gold bars, coke and cut gems; the coast gives fish, shells, pearls, oil, ink and jade.
  • Cross-collect & upgrade blooms — adjacency bonuses and tier-up tiles squeeze extra yield out of the same drag.
  • Crafting stations — Bakery, Larder, Forge, Kitchen, Smokehouse and Workshop convert raw goods into far more valuable finished ones.
  • Building & worker bonuses — a Sawmill adds planks, an Apiary adds honey, a Powder Store stamps out bombs, line-workers thin the chain you need.
  • Special tiles — Mysterious Ore and the Giant Pearl mint runes; coin-tiles mint coins.

Sinks — where resources are spent

  • Crafting — every finished good eats its raw inputs (3 flour + 1 egg per loaf, and so on).
  • Constructing buildings — each plot costs a basket of goods (planks, bread, blocks, iron…), never just coins.
  • Tier-ups — leveling the town itself demands a bigger basket each rung (a City wants 28 bread, 14 pies, 18 eggs, and more).
  • Tools — crafted from raw materials and spent in a single use.
  • Expedition fuel — mine and harbor runs are paid in food: supplies, cured meat and iron rations baked back home are spent as turns.
  • Orders & festivals — goods handed to neighbors, and the great larder filled for the finale, leave your stores for good.
The loop that ties the whole kingdom together

Notice how the sinks reach across zones. You bake bread on the farm so you can afford the turns to dig in the mountain; you mine iron so you can build the Forge that makes the goods you sell; you sell at market for the coins that found the coast. No single board can sustain itself — the economy is deliberately woven so that every land you raise feeds the next.

8 The Keeper at the fence

The first reckoning

Every settlement has a Keeper — an old guardian spirit of the land — and once your home has grown enough, yours comes to the fence to be reckoned with. This is the first real choice of the game: coexist with the Keeper or drive it out. Either way, you settle the question, and in doing so you complete your home settlement and are handed your first Hearth Token — a heirloom seed, proof that one corner of the old kingdom lives again. Wren tells you the road beyond has opened. Act one is done.

9 The world opens

The caravan and the map

To leave the Vale you outfit a caravan, paid for in coins, and roll it out onto the map — a hand-drawn world of regions beyond your own: the Greenfields, the Wilds, the Stoneholds, the Coast, and far off, the Old Capital. The smith Bram arrives, asking for a forge; once you build it and craft your first iron, the Vale has metal, and harder country becomes worth visiting. Sister Liss follows, a healer for the road ahead.

Two farms to learn the rhythm

Your first steps out are gentle: Greenmeadow and the Wild Orchard are farm towns like home — same green logic of grass and grain and fruit, same 50-coin toll — so you find your feet before the world gets stranger.

10 Three realms, three tokens

Every land grows differently

The heart of the long game is that each kind of settlement plays by its own rules, with its own tiles, its own resources kept in its own stores, its own tools, its own Keeper, and its own token at the end. The old kingdom needs one of each restored.

The Stoneholds — mining

At the Cracked Quarry and the Lanternlit Caves you work stone and ore instead of grass: blocks, iron and copper bars, coke, cut gems, silver and gold. The boards are deeper and slower to tame — you bring drills, stone hammers and gold picks, pump out lava and blast through cave-ins — and you spend food from home as your turns. The tier ladder runs long: Dig Site, Prospect Camp, Mining Camp, Boomtown, Smeltworks, all the way to a roaring Foundry City, with each rung asking for heavier metal than the last.

The Coast — fishing

At Saltspray Harbor and Mirefen Hollow the water itself moves: tides roll in and out across your turns, changing what the board offers. You pull fish, shellfish, pearls, oil and ink from a stilt-village that grows out over the marsh — Fishing Stilt to Bogwalk Hamlet to Mire Village to Fen Town — and here the tier-ups are paid in the sea's own bounty: fillets, oil, and at the end a pearl.

Around all this sits the warm machinery of a settled world: the worker economy, daily quests and an almanac that tracks your mastery, decorations to make a town yours, market stalls, and small personal story arcs with the people who've joined you. You move between Town, Inventory, Craft, Map, and Townsfolk as the mood takes you. But the spine of it is always the same: grow a settlement of each kind until its Keeper is reckoned with and its token is yours.

11 The Harvest Festival

The Vale lives again

Back home, the work pays off in the warmest beat of the game. With every building raised and the festival larder filled — heaps of the staples that carried you here, the grass and wheat and flour and berries and oak — Hearthwood Vale throws a Harvest Festival. The place you found cold and silent is now loud with people and full of food. This is the emotional summit of the settled world: not a number going up, but a home that is unmistakably, finally, alive.

12 The Old Capital

The road the whole journey pointed at

With every settlement restored and all three Hearth Tokens in hand — seed, iron, and pearl, the farm, the mountain, and the sea — the last road on the map opens. The Old Capital has stood locked and waiting at the far edge of the world this entire time, and only a kingdom made whole again can reach it. You take the caravan out one final time, past every town you raised from nothing, to the seat of the old realm — and there the journey that began at one cold hearth comes to its rest. The Vale, the fields, the stoneholds, the coast, and the capital: lit again, together. That is the run.

And then, if you like, you simply keep tending the world you brought back — a cozy place to return to, season after season.
