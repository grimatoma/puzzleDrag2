What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and you outfit a caravan to roll out and found new towns across the map, opening and developing several at your own pace. Each town stacks a new board on the last and grows bigger and harder, but the heart never changes: drag, harvest, grow, complete, move on.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm, build a caravan, and head out across the map to found new towns — a mining valley, a hazard-ridden quarry, a harbor, a rat-plagued meadow, a deep dark delve — until you raise the Old Capital and wake the kingdom.

The core loop

Everything is one compounding loop: play the board to harvest; spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; the next round is bigger. Every system in this set either feeds that flywheel or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — your first chain to your last monument.

Boards build on each other

The backbone — and what keeps it from being "the same board over and over": a new board type is always fed by the boards before it. The farm makes food; food buys mine expeditions; farm food (bait) plus mine metal (tackle) fund the fishery. So every town with a mine also has a farm, and every fishery town has a farm and a mine. The one deliberate exception is Deepdelve — a mine with no farm; see The seven towns.

The puzzles stack, and earlier boards never idle — later boards keep buying from them.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board: a small budget of turns (~10), each chain spends one, whatever you harvest banks into that town's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Each town is a long project, not a handful of sessions — finishing one takes real effort. But it is effort-gated, not time-gated: you are never stuck waiting on a clock. The time between sessions hands you free tools (timed depots, daily, civic) to go faster — or, if you would rather not wait, you craft those tools now by spending extra materials.

The long arc is a map of seven towns, all required for the finale but openable in any order the map allows. You found each by caravan, build it up, and beat its signature task — but you need not finish one before opening the next: several towns can be in progress at once and you switch between them freely. Difficulty is cozy early, ramping late: the early sites are forgiving, and the rising founding cost plus the map's outward-expanding reach keep the harder, farther sites for later — a cozy unfurling, not a forced order. Workers, coins, tools and Villagers travel with you; each town's materials are its own.
