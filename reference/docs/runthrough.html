From a Cold Hearth to the Old Capital

one full playthrough, told as a story

This is a single, start-to-finish walk through the game — not a manual and not a wiki, just the shape of one complete run: what you see, what you do, and how a dead little settlement grows back into a living kingdom. Read it top to bottom the way you'd play it.

1 The opening

A cold hearth

You arrive at Hearthwood Vale — your home — and it is quiet and cold. The hearth at its center has gone out. An old caretaker, Wren, presses a pair of iron tongs into your hands, asks you to name the place, and tells you the truth of it plainly: light the hearth, and the Vale begins to wake. Everything else follows from that first warmth.

You name your settlement. The board slides up in front of you. You make your first drag — and the game has begun.

2 How the board plays

Drag, chain, collect

The board is a small grid of living tiles — tufts of grass, stalks of grain, trees, birds. You play by pressing on a tile and dragging across its neighbors: every tile of the same kind that you touch joins a chain behind your finger, snaking in any of the eight directions. Drag through three or more and the chain lights up; let go, and it collects.

Collecting does three things at once. The tiles you gathered turn into a resource dropped into your stores — grass becomes hay, grain becomes flour, trees become planks, birds become eggs, fruit becomes pie. If the chain was long enough to cross a threshold, a higher upgrade tile blooms where you finished, so good chains seed better boards. And any partner tiles sitting right beside your chain get swept up too — a cross-collect bonus that rewards reading the whole board, not just one line. Then everything above slides down to fill the gaps and fresh tiles drop in from the top.

The rhythm of a session

Each visit to a board is a handful of turns — roughly ten. As your turns burn down, the seasons drift across the board behind your play: spring greens into summer, ambers into autumn, then settles toward winter. It's the visual heartbeat of a run. When the turns are gone, a harvest summary tallies what you made and you head back to town. What you're really chasing the whole time: longer chains, clever cross-collect adjacencies, and not wasting turns.

3 Lighting the hearth

The first harvest, and a town that grows

Your first goal is small and warm: gather enough hay to light the hearth. You harvest your first tile, then twenty bundles of hay, and the fire catches — Mira arrives to keep you company, and soon Old Tomas too. Now the loop opens up. Neighbors post orders — bring me bread, bring me soup — and filling them pays coins, the one currency that follows you everywhere. You craft raw resources into finished goods at your stations (the mill grinds flour, the bakery bakes bread), and you raise your first real building: the granary, room for tomorrow.

Spend coins on hands that ease the work

Those coins go furthest on workers. Some are paid to make a crop need a shorter chain — hire a grain hand and a run of wheat that used to demand six tiles to pay off now rewards you at five, so every drag does more and the board feels looser. Others cut your crafting costs, so the same bread takes less flour. Hiring takes a coin and a villager (your housing decides how many hands you can keep), and it's one of the main ways an early grind softens into an easy, generous rhythm — you're quietly buying the work down.

As you raise buildings, the town itself levels up beneath you — from a bare Outpost to a Hamlet, then a Village, then a full City. Each tier-up costs a basket of goods you've been making, and each one widens the town: more plots to build on, and a longer list of buildings you're allowed to raise. By the time Hearthwood Vale is a City it sprawls across twenty plots, loud and lived-in — the cold ruin you started in, grown all the way up.

You can't build everything — so choose

Here's the catch that gives the town its character: at every tier you unlock far more buildings than you have plots for. The little Outpost lets you pick three buildings from five; the City unlocks close to thirty buildings but only ever has twenty plots. So you're always choosing what your town is for — lean into baking and brewing, or sawmills and a forge, or housing and workshops? Plots are precious, and what you leave out matters as much as what you build. Later you can knock things down and re-plan, but the squeeze never fully goes away, and that's the point: the town is a series of small, real decisions, not a checklist.

4 The Keeper at the fence

The first reckoning

Every settlement has a Keeper — an old guardian spirit of the land — and once your home has grown enough, yours comes to the fence to be reckoned with. This is the first real choice of the game: coexist with the Keeper or drive it out. Either way, you settle the question, and in doing so you complete your home settlement and are handed your first Hearth Token — a heirloom seed, proof that one corner of the old kingdom lives again. Wren tells you the road beyond has opened. Act one is done.

5 The world opens

The caravan and the map

To leave the Vale you outfit a caravan, paid for in coins, and roll it out onto the map — a hand-drawn world of regions beyond your own: the Greenfields, the Wilds, the Stoneholds, the Coast, and far off, the Old Capital. Founding a new town here is non-linear: you can settle any site the map lets you reach and that you can afford, and the cost climbs with each town you open. You never have to finish one town before starting the next — you can have several in progress at once and switch between them freely, leaving a place that isn't grabbing you and coming back later.

The smith Bram arrives, asking for a forge; once you build it and craft your first iron, the Vale has metal, and harder country becomes worth visiting. Sister Liss follows, a healer for the road ahead.

Two farms to learn the rhythm

Your first steps out are gentle: Greenmeadow and the Wild Orchard are farm towns like home — same green logic of grass and grain and fruit — so you find your feet before the world gets stranger.

6 Three realms, three tokens

Every land grows differently

The heart of the long game is that each kind of settlement plays by its own rules, and the old kingdom needs one of each restored. Each is its own board, its own resources kept in its own stores, its own Keeper, and its own token at the end.

The Stoneholds — mining

At the Cracked Quarry and the Lanternlit Caves you work stone and ore instead of grass: blocks, iron and copper bars, coke, cut gems, silver and gold. The boards are deeper and slower to tame, and the tier ladder runs long — Dig Site, Prospect Camp, Mining Camp, Boomtown, Smeltworks, all the way to a roaring Foundry City.

The Coast — fishing

At Saltspray Harbor and Mirefen Hollow the water itself moves: tides roll in and out across your turns, changing what the board offers. You pull fish, shellfish, pearls and oil from a stilt-village that grows out over the marsh — Fishing Stilt to Bogwalk Hamlet to Mire Village to Fen Town.

Around all this sits the warm machinery of a settled world: the worker economy, daily quests and an almanac that tracks your mastery, decorations to make a town yours, market stalls, and small personal story arcs with the people who've joined you. You move between Town, Inventory, Craft, Map, and Townsfolk as the mood takes you. But the spine of it is always the same: grow a settlement of each kind until its Keeper is reckoned with and its token is yours.

7 The Harvest Festival

The Vale lives again

Back home, the work pays off in the warmest beat of the game. With every building raised and the festival larder filled — fifty each of the staples that carried you here, the grass and wheat and flour and berries and oak — Hearthwood Vale throws a Harvest Festival. The place you found cold and silent is now loud with people and full of food. This is the emotional summit of the settled world: not a number going up, but a home that is unmistakably, finally, alive.

8 The Old Capital

The road the whole journey pointed at

With every settlement restored and all three Hearth Tokens in hand — seed, iron, and pearl, the farm, the mountain, and the sea — the last road on the map opens. The Old Capital has stood locked and waiting at the far edge of the world this entire time, and only a kingdom made whole again can reach it. You take the caravan out one final time, past every town you raised from nothing, to the seat of the old realm — and there the journey that began at one cold hearth comes to its rest. The Vale, the fields, the stoneholds, the coast, and the capital: lit again, together. That is the run.
