What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and once it's thriving and you've completed its signature task, you load a wagon and roll out to found the next town. Each town is a fresh board with its own twist and its own win condition, but always the same heart: drag, harvest, grow, complete, move on.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm, throw the first harvest festival, then pack a wagon and head over the ridge to found the next town — a quarry, a harbor, a rat-plagued meadow — each harder and larger than the last, until you raise the Old Capital and wake the whole kingdom.

The core loop

Everything is one compounding loop: play the board to harvest; spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; the next round is bigger. Every system in this doc either feeds that flywheel or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — your first chain to your last monument.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board: a small budget of turns (~10), each chain spends one, whatever you harvest banks into that town's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Between visits you spend the stockpile on growth.

The long arc is a ladder of towns. You found each one by wagon, build it up, beat its signature task, and move on — towns getting bigger and harder as you climb. Workers, coins, and your global tool kit travel with you; each town's resource stockpile is its own. The whole game is that ladder, ending at the Old Capital.

The board & the chain

The board is a 6×6 grid of tiles from a few families. You play by dragging through a run of touching, same-family tiles — three or more, in any of 8 directions, no reuse. Release and the chain resolves; the board collapses and refills; one turn is spent.

Every chain does two things at once — the core decision of the game:
  • It harvests — a chain of N tiles banks floor(N ÷ divisor) of that resource. Longer chain → more goods.
  • It merges — a long-enough chain promotes a tile up its ladder (grass→bird, grain→vegetable…). Longer chain → a better board.
You can't max both at once. Reading the board for the best chain is the puzzle.

Tiers & divisors. Each family climbs a tier ladder; higher tiers yield more. The divisor sets how generous a family is — small for staples (grass), big for premium goods (flowers, gems, gold) — which keeps hay common and a cut gem precious. Refining turns raw harvest into the goods the game actually prices in: Mill→flour, Bakery→bread, Kitchen→supplies, Forge→bars & tools.

Founding by wagon

Every town past your home is founded by sending out a wagon — the universal "open the next place" ritual. You complete the current town's signature task, that unlocks the wagon route, and you spend a founding cost to roll out and settle the next site. Home is the one exception: it's where you start.

today founding cost already exists (~300 × 1.7ⁿ coins, escalating per town) and the map tracks visited/adjacent nodes. design wrap that in the wagon as a single, legible founding act, gated on the previous town's win — and (for later towns) let the wagon also carry a starter basket of supplies for the journey.

Workers — the multiplier (and the main cost)

You hire workers with coins + resources. Each hire permanently lowers production cost (fewer tiles per unit) or adds an ability (promote a category, cut a recipe's cost, bonus coins/runes). Workers are global — they follow you to every town — so they're the engine that makes the flywheel accelerate across the whole run.

Worker hiring is also the game's main sink, and the design wants it to bite harder so growth stays meaningful: scale both the coin and the material cost up, and steeper.

Some workers use a geometric coin curve already (Baker 75 × 1.4ⁿ); most are linear. Max 10 per type. Today the material is a flat 2 units that only steps every 3rd hire — that's the part the design most wants to steepen.

Tools & the building → tool unlock

Tools are board powers you craft and spend — clear a tile, shuffle, drop a bomb, reveal ore, clear a hazard. Your tool inventory is global today (one kit across every town).

The design ties tools to buildings: building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted. So your town layout literally decides what's in your kit. today the seed of this already exists — the Town Hall's "provisions" map buildings to tools (Mill→clear, Workshop→basic, Forge→rare, Granary→shuffle, Sawmill→axe, Powder Store→bomb) and Powder Store actually grants its tool — and ~30 tools have craft recipes. design make that the rule across the board, and gate each recipe behind its building so unlocking is meaningful. The full map is on the Buildings & tools tab.

Expeditions, hazards & the supply system

Some boards push back. Expedition boards (mine, harbor) make you pack food to buy turns: spend rations to depart, play till they run out, and keep everything you gathered (soft fail, never a wipe). The food→turns rate is the single biggest pacing dial — it sets how much farming funds one trip down. Hazards (cave-ins, gas, moles; rats; storms) eat tiles or block you and force a response — the urgency a gentle farm never has.

The supply system — for towns with no farm. A farm town makes its own food; a mine-only town can't. design there, you provision two ways: supply depots — buildings that generate a small, capped number of supplies every few real-time hours — and shipping a basket in from your farm towns by wagon. Both are limited, so the town becomes a planning puzzle: ration scarce supplies across expeditions instead of farming freely. today neither the real-time drip nor inter-zone shipping exists (the closest is the 8-hour Town-Hall tax); this is the signature new system for the mine-only town.

The economy: value from faucets to sinks

The whole game is a tank. Faucets pour value in (mostly the board, plus a ring of always-on systems). Sinks drain it out (everything you spend to grow or to open the next town). The design lives in the loop: the biggest sinks pour back into the faucets, so spending on growth grows production.

The three loops that matter

1 · Reinvestment (the flywheel)

Board → resources → tile upgrades + worker hires → the board yields more → repeat. The engine of the whole game; it compounds and never stops. Scaling worker cost is how we keep it from trivialising itself.

2 · Growth

Resources → buildings + town tiers → more plots, new tools, new tile families → which add new faucets and sinks. How a town gets wider, not just faster.

3 · The provisioning spine

Food → expedition turns → ore → refine → build → capacity. In farm+mine towns the farm pays for it; in mine-only towns the supply drip + shipping pay for it under scarcity.

The shape of the whole thing (and its tension)

The economy is lopsided — on purpose, but it needs watching. Faucets are abundant (the board plus daily / civic / quests / achievements / bosses). Sinks are resource-gated — buildings want big baskets, worker hires want coins and escalating resources (more so once we scale them). So difficulty lives in the sinks. One consequence to keep flagged: coins are an easy faucet with thin sinks (tiers/buildings are resource-priced; the Market doesn't buy yet), so "what are coins for?" is the biggest open balance question — see Gaps.

The town path

The game is a ladder of towns, each founded by wagon, each with one unique hook, one crux (the thing it teaches), and one win condition — a signature "big task" that needn't be a boss. Towns get bigger and harder as you climb. The three biome towns (farm, mine, fish) each yield a hearth token, and the three tokens open the finale.

Sizes are the design target (T1 deliberately tiny, the finale largest). Authored maps exist today at home 3→20, quarry 2→12, mirefen 3→15 plots — these can be re-sized / re-skinned to fit the ladder.

Town 1 Hearthwood Vale — the farm specified to numbers

Crux
The tutorial hub. Teaches every verb — chain, refine, fill orders, tier up, hire a worker — with no pressure. Stays your home and caravan terminus forever.
Unique hook
None by design: it's the gentle teacher. Seasons turn the board over, but nothing attacks you.
Size
~6 plots (deliberately tiny — re-sized down from the authored 20-plot home map). A handful of buildings, no sprawl.
Buildings
Hearth (free) · Mill · Bakery · Granary · Kitchen · one of Inn/Larder. The bread + supplies chain and basic storage — nothing more.
Win task
Host the Harvest Festival — gather a festival basket of farm goods (bread, eggs, a luxury). Completing it grants the farm hearth token and unlocks the wagon to found Town 2.
  1. First chains. Drag 3+ touching tiles; each chain banks crops and, if long, merges a tile up a tier. One of ~10 turns.
  2. Refine. Grain → Mill → flour → Bakery → bread, the staple everything is priced in. Granary stores it; the Kitchen turns grain into supplies for later.
  3. Earn & shape. Fill villager orders for coins; hire your first worker (cheaper forever, travels with you). Quests/achievements tick in the background.
  4. Throw the festival. Stockpile the festival basket, host the Harvest Festival — the gentle "you've learned it" moment — and pack the wagon for the ridge.

Town 2 Cracked Quarry — the combination specified to numbers

Crux
The supply chain made literal: a farm board (normal turns) grows food; the food buys descents into a mine board (expeditions); the mine pays out ore. Two boards, one shared stockpile — one economy literally funding the other.
Unique hook
Two boards in one town, and mine hazards (cave-ins, gas vents, moles) that the farm never had.
Size
~12 plots (the authored 6-tier quarry ladder, 2→12). Founded by wagon, gated until home reached City.
Buildings
Adds the mine line: Mining Camp · Workshop · Forge · Powder Store · Barn (mine-board preserve) — plus the carried farm buildings. This is where most tools come online (picks, hammers, bomb).
Win task
Defeat the capstone boss — the guardian in the deep (e.g. Old Stoneface). Grants the mine hearth token and the wagon onward.
  1. Found & bootstrap. Wagon to the pit; workers/tools came with you, the stockpile starts empty.
  2. Run the farm board. Free turns: chain grain, build the Kitchen, convert grain → supplies.
  3. Pack & descend. Spend supplies to depart a mine expedition; chain stone/iron while hazards press you; run out of food and you keep everything mined.
  4. Build the mine up. Spend ore to climb the ladder, unlock Forge + metal tools, hire mine workers. Farm refills the pantry; the mine empties it.
  5. Beat the guardian. Stock the boss basket, win the fight, take the token, pack the wagon.

Towns 3–6 — the full path (big-picture; numbers later)

Town 3 Saltspray Harbor — the fishery

Crux
Expeditions move to the water. Same pack-food-for-turns spine as the mine, but read against tides — a second take on provisioning before the game gets mean about it.
Unique hook
The sea: tide states shift the board, and storms are the hazard. Fish refine at the Smokehouse/Fishmonger.
Size
~15 plots (mirefen's authored 4-tier ladder fits, or a harbor re-skin).
Win task
The Regatta — a festival that asks for a haul of cured fish + a built dock, rather than a fight (variety from Town 2). Grants the fish hearth token — the third, which opens the finale.

Town 4 Deepdelve — the mine with no farm

Crux
The planning town. With no farm board, you can't freely make food — so every expedition is rationed against a limited supply income. Progress is about sequencing, not grinding.
Unique hook
The supply system: supply depots generate a small capped batch every few real-time hours, and you can ship a basket in by wagon from your farm towns. Both scarce on purpose.
Size
~16 plots (procedural map, or the Black Forge / Caves layouts).
Win task
Fill the great shipment — accumulate and dispatch a large ore order (a "big task" that isn't a boss or a party). Proves you mastered scarce provisioning.

Town 5 Greenmeadow — the rich farm & the rats

Crux
The loop turns on you: abundance is the threat. As the stockpile climbs, rats appear and eat adjacent crops each turn — farm-side hazard management mirroring the mine's danger.
Unique hook
The rat hazard and its counters: chain rats directly, build the Ratcatcher (free moves on rats) and Master Ratcatcher (clear rats together with grass).
Size
~18 plots — the widest farm yet.
Win task
Raise a great building — a Cathedral / Great Granary that demands a deep, sustained resource basket while you keep the rats down.

Town 6 The Old Capital — the finale

Crux
Everything at once. Gated behind the three hearth tokens (farm + mine + fish), it's the capstone that asks you to bring the whole toolkit to bear.
Unique hook
A civic build with mixed demands drawing on every prior town's goods (and, if we want, multiple hazards).
Size
The biggest layout (the 20-plot City map fits the role).
Win task
Build the grand monument — the ending. The longest basket in the game; finishing it wins the run.
Why the win tasks vary. Festival (T1), boss (T2), regatta (T3), shipment (T4), great building (T5), monument (T6) — so "completing a town" never feels like one rote beat. today completion is "build enough + resolve the town's keeper (coexist/drive-out) → hearth token." design reframes that keeper-resolution moment as the town's signature task, and varies it per town.

Buildings unlock tools — globally

A town's plots are scarce, and what you build on them decides your kit: building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted for the rest of the run. Plots are few in Town 1 and grow with each town, so early on you're choosing which tools to bring online — a real decision, not a checklist.

today tools are already a global inventory; ~30 tools have craft recipes; and the Town Hall's "provisions" already map buildings to tools (Mill→clear, Workshop→basic, Forge→rare, Granary→shuffle, Sawmill→axe, Powder Store→bomb), with Powder Store fully granting its tool. design make "build unlocks the recipe" the universal rule, using the map below.

The point of small early plots. With ~6 plots in Town 1, you can't have everything — so unlocking the Granary (shuffle) instead of, say, a yield building is a felt choice. As towns widen, the roster fills out and the kit deepens. Plot scarcity is the early-game's main strategic texture.

The systems that wrap the loop

Around the board sits a ring of always-on systems. None is the game, but each shapes how it feels — pacing a session, rewarding a return, sequencing the story, compounding your power. What feeds each, and what it feeds back:

Workers — the multiplier

Coins + (scaling) resources in; permanently cheaper production + abilities out. Global. The accelerator of the flywheel and the biggest sink — see Mechanics for the cost direction.

Story — the sequencer

Thresholds (a total, a building, a win task) in; the next town, boss summons, rare resources out. Decides when each part of the game opens — the order behind the verbs.

Achievements

Passive counters in; mostly coins, a few special tools out. One-way faucet rewarding mastery & breadth.

Quests

A handful of seasonal goals in; coins + XP out. Gives a session a target beyond "play till turns end."

Daily login

Showing up in; a streak of coins + tools out. Pure retention, off to the side.

Civic tax & supply drip

Time + how much you've built in; coins + tool provisions (a board crate) out — and, in mine-only towns, the supply drip. The game's idle-ish layer.

Tools

Fed by nearly everything (daily, civic, achievements, building grants) and unlocked by buildings; spent as board powers. Global. The shared "spend" that ties the side-faucets to play.

Bosses → runes → portal

Beating a boss pays coins + a rune; runes are meant to fuel portal summons — still being wired (see Gaps). Today runes mostly accumulate.

One pattern to notice: most side systems pay out in coins — fine only if coins have somewhere meaningful to go, which they mostly don't yet. The meta ring and the "what are coins for?" question are the same problem from two sides.
