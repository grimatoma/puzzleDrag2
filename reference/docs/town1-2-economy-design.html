What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and once it's thriving and you've completed its signature task, you load a caravan and roll out to found the next town. Each town stacks a new board on the last and gets bigger and harder, but always the same heart: drag, harvest, grow, complete, move on.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm, build a caravan, and head over the ridge to found the next town — a mining valley, a hazard-ridden quarry, a harbor, a rat-plagued meadow, a deep dark delve — each larger, until you raise the Old Capital and wake the kingdom.

The core loop

Everything is one compounding loop: play the board to harvest; spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; the next round is bigger. Every system in this doc either feeds that flywheel or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — your first chain to your last monument.

Boards build on each other

The backbone — and what keeps it from being "the same board over and over": a new board type is always fed by the boards before it. The farm makes food; food buys mine expeditions; farm food (bait) + mine metal (tackle) fund the fishery. So every town with a mine also has a farm, and every fishery town has a farm and a mine.

The puzzles stack, and earlier boards never idle — later boards keep buying from them.

One deliberate exception. Deepdelve (Town 6) is a mine with no farm — the stacking rule switched off on purpose. With no local food engine it forces a different game: you ration supplies that trickle in from timed depots and arrive by caravan shipment from your farm towns. The exception that makes the rule felt.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board: a small budget of turns (~10), each chain spends one, whatever you harvest banks into that town's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Each town is a long project, not a handful of sessions locked — finishing one takes real effort. But it's effort-gated, not time-gated: you're never stuck waiting on a clock. The time between sessions hands you free tools (timed depots, daily, civic) to go faster — or, if you'd rather not wait, you craft those tools now by spending extra materials (more farming). Wait for free, or pay in farming to push ahead.

The long arc is a ladder of seven towns, all required. You found each by caravan, build it up, beat its signature task, and roll on. Difficulty is cozy → ramps late locked: the first towns are forgiving; hazards and scarcity bite in the back half. Workers, coins, tools and Villagers travel with you; each town's materials are its own.

The board & the chain

The board is a 6×6 grid of tiles from a few families. You play by dragging through a run of touching, same-family tiles — three or more, in any of 8 directions, no reuse. Release and the chain resolves; the board collapses and refills; one turn is spent.

Every chain does two things at once — the core decision of the game:
  • It harvests — a chain of N tiles banks floor(N ÷ divisor) of that material. Longer chain → more goods.
  • It merges — a long-enough chain promotes a tile up its ladder (grass→bird, grain→vegetable…). Longer chain → a better board.
You can't max both at once. Reading the board for the best chain is the puzzle.

The two chain knobs (kept deep)

locked: keep the depth. Every chain is read by two independent length gates, and that's where the long-game optimization lives:

Both start high and workers buy them down toward a floor. Cheap staples (grass, grain) hit the floor with a few hires; premium lines (flowers, gems, gold) start far higher and need many workers and time — so the accelerator is real but self-limiting. This is the depth knob behind the whole production curve. today the divisor scales & is worker-reducible; the floor (currently 1) and the upgrade-threshold buy-down are the build.

Researching new tiles

locked direction. The board's vocabulary grows over the run: you research, buy, or earn new tile types, each with its own unique ability (a special yield, a chain effect, a hazard-clearer, a wildcard). They arrive from several places — a research track (spend materials + time), the coin "unlock a tile" sink, or rewards (achievements, win-tasks, boss/festival drops). An unlock is permanent and global, so your kit of board pieces deepens alongside your buildings & tools — a third axis of identity.

Why it matters. New tiles keep the 6×6 puzzle fresh as the towns stack, and give coins + materials a meaningful, non-grindy sink (one-time unlocks, not a treadmill). today tiles & families exist in code; design the research/acquire layer + per-tile abilities is the build.

Founding by caravan

You leave every town the same way: build a caravan and roll it out to found the next site. Town 1's whole win condition is building that first caravan; thereafter each town's signature task unlocks the caravan onward, and founding spends a (rising) coin cost for the journey. Home is where you start; every town after is caravan-founded.

Workers, Villagers & Houses

Workers are the multiplier: each hire permanently buys down the chain knobs (above) or adds an ability. They're global — they follow you to every town. But hiring is gated by two things now:

Villagers — the worker currency

Your population. Houses produce Villagers; hiring a worker spends a Villager (plus a materials cost). So your worker count is capped by how many Houses you've raised.

Houses cost slots

A House takes a building slot like anything else — so more workers means fewer ability/yield buildings. That tension (population vs. specialization) is a core build decision.

Hire cost = Villager + materials

locked Hiring is not a coin sink. It spends a Villager and a (scaling) basket of materials; the basket steepens with each hire so late workers are a real investment.

Removing buildings locked — tear down a discretionary building to re-plan a town. Two limits: major buildings are permanent (the Hearth, board-openers like Mine Head / Wharf, a town's signature, and anything that unlocked a new functionality can't be removed once built); and you can't remove a House if it would drop Villagers below the number in use by hired workers — build one elsewhere first (the pool is global). design Building upgrades (levelling a building for a stronger effect) are a parked, later concept.

Tools & the building → tool unlock

Tools are board powers you craft and spend — clear a tile, shuffle, drop a bomb, find ore, clear a hazard. Your tool inventory is global today (one kit across every town).

The design ties tools to buildings: building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted — and a few buildings generate a tool on a timer. So your town layout decides your kit. today the seed exists (the Town Hall maps buildings to tools, Powder Store actually grants its bomb, ~30 tools have recipes); design makes "build unlocks the recipe / timed grant" universal. Full map on Buildings & tools.

Expeditions, hazards & timed supply

Expedition boards (mine, fishery) make you pack what an earlier board produced to buy turns: spend farm food (and, for the fishery, mine metal) to depart, play till it runs out, and keep everything you gathered (soft fail, never a wipe). That exchange rate is the single biggest pacing dial.

Hazards arrive gradually (cozy → ramps): none in the first two towns, then cave-ins/gas/moles, tides, rats, and combinations — they eat tiles or block you and force a response.

Timed generation = a helper, never a gate. locked Some buildings generate a small, capped batch every few hours — supplies or a tool — the free way to speed up between sessions. But nothing is time-locked: if you don't want to wait, craft the same tool now by spending materials (extra farming). It's the spine of the farm-less Deepdelve (its Supply Depot) and a gentle boost everywhere else.

The currency model locked

Three currencies, deliberately unequal — so your farming has to be targeted, not just "earn the catch-all and buy anything."

Materials — the main currency

Per-town, board-grown (crops, ore, fish & their refined goods). They pay for buildings, win-tasks, expedition rations, refining, and the worker-hire basket. They shift town to town, so each town's farming is purposeful — there's no super-currency that buys everything.

Coins — narrow & soft

locked Coins do a small, specific job: town-tier upgrades and unlocking tiles (and the caravan journey). Deliberately not a powerful blocker — the farm is never coin-gated, and you can't buy materials with them.

Villagers — worker currency

Population from Houses; spent to hire workers (see Mechanics). A constrained, valuable resource — and the one optional-IAP lever (below).

Why materials > coins. If one catch-all currency bought everything, farming would be undirected and a big coin pile would trivialise progress. Making materials the real cost means you farm for a reason — this building, that win-task — and coins stay a light convenience. Progression should feel like an accomplishment, not a wallet check.

The three loops that matter

1 · Reinvestment (flywheel)

Board → materials → tile upgrades + workers → the board yields more. The engine; it compounds. Steeper worker baskets + the chain-knob floors keep it from trivialising itself.

2 · Growth

Materials → buildings (+ Houses for Villagers) → more plots, new tools, new families. How a town gets wider — and where the build-choice strategy lives.

3 · Provisioning

Food → expedition turns → ore/fish → refine → build. In Deepdelve, timed depots + shipping replace the farm under scarcity.

The seven-town path

Seven towns, all required, each founded by caravan. Every town adds at most one new board (Farm → Mine → Fish), one crux, one win task (varied — not always a boss), and a thematic signature building (some exportable to other towns once you finish — so unlock order matters).

Sizes are the design target. Authored maps exist today at home 3→20, quarry 2→12, mirefen 3→15 plots — re-sizable / re-skinnable to fit.

Common, specialized & clear-named

locked direction. Two kinds of buildings, and every name should say what it does:

  • Common — the board's core chain (Mill, Bakery, Smelter, Wharf…), available in any town with that board. These are the must-builds.
  • Specialized — one thematic signature per town, tied to its core idea. Once that town is complete, its signature can be raised in your other towns for a cross-zone bonus — so unlock order is strategy.
Slots < buildings — and tightening locked. After the core chain, only a few discretionary slots remain, and Houses compete for them too (more Villagers vs. more abilities). The gap widens as towns grow — slots lag the building menu — so later towns force harder, sharper specialization. Because a building unlocks its tool globally & permanently, the choice is about pacing & per-town identity, not permanent loss. Discretionary buildings can be removed to re-plan — but major / functionality-unlocking buildings are permanent once built (and a House can't drop below workers in use). Houses, Villagers & removal

Houses → Villagers

Each House adds Villager capacity (a global pool). You spend Villagers to hire workers — so population gates your worker count.

The slot tension

A House costs a slot, so it competes with ability/yield buildings. Wide worker base, or rich abilities? — a real per-town call.

Removal rule

Tear down discretionary buildings to re-plan — but major / functionality-unlocking buildings are permanent, and a House can't go if it would push Villagers below those in use.

The systems that wrap the loop

Around the board sits a ring of always-on systems. None is the game, but each shapes how it feels.

Workers + Villagers

Houses make Villagers; Villagers + materials hire workers; workers buy down the chain knobs. The flywheel's accelerator (see Mechanics).

Story — the sequencer

Thresholds (a total, a building, a win task) unlock the next town, boss summons, rare materials. Decides when each part opens.

Achievements

Passive counters → mostly coins, a few special tools. One-way faucet rewarding mastery & breadth.

Quests

Seasonal goals → coins + XP. A target beyond "play till turns end."

Daily login

A streak of coins + tools. Pure retention.

Civic tax & timed depots

Time + how much you've built → coins + tool provisions + the idle ration drip. The day-cadence layer.

Tools

Unlocked by buildings, fed by daily/civic/depots; spent as board powers. Global.

Bosses → runes

Beating a boss pays coins + a rune. With the finale gated on all towns, what runes/tokens buy is an open question (see Gaps).

Monetization — optional jumps, never gates locked direction

The game wants real play and a bit of grind — progression should feel like an accomplishment. Monetization is therefore an optional accelerator, never a forced wall: it lets a player skip some grind, not buy the win.

Buy Villagers

Extra worker currency (as PC2 sold) — jump your worker count without grinding Houses. Optional; Houses remain the free path.

Toolcrate drop

A bundle of random tools — a convenience boost, not power you can't otherwise craft.

Speed-ups (maybe)

Skip a timed-depot cooldown or a founding wait. Pure time-save; the idle layer already gives it free, slower.

The rule: every IAP must be earnable by playing. We sell time and convenience, not gates. If a thing can only be bought, it's the wrong thing to sell.
