What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and once it's thriving and you've completed its signature task, you load a caravan and roll out to found the next town. Each town stacks a new board on the last and gets bigger and harder, but always the same heart: drag, harvest, grow, complete, move on.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm, build a caravan, and head over the ridge to found the next town — first a quiet farm-and-mine valley, then a hazard-ridden quarry, a harbor, a rat-plagued meadow — each one larger, until you raise the Old Capital and wake the whole kingdom.

The core loop

Everything is one compounding loop: play the board to harvest; spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; the next round is bigger. Every system in this doc either feeds that flywheel or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — your first chain to your last monument.

Boards build on each other

The backbone of the whole progression — and the thing that keeps it from being "the same board over and over": a new board type is always fed by the boards before it.

So every town with a mine also has a farm; every town with a fishery has a farm and a mine. The puzzles stack, and earlier boards never idle — later boards keep buying from them.

Each town in the path adds at most one new board on top of the stack it inherited. The whole game is that stack growing — farm, then farm+mine, then farm+mine+fish — across a ladder of towns.

One deliberate exception. Deepdelve (Town 6) is a mine with no farm — the stacking rule switched off on purpose. With no local food engine it forces a different game: you ration supplies that trickle in from timed depots and arrive by caravan shipment from your farm towns. It's the rule's exception precisely so the rule is felt.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board: a small budget of turns (~10), each chain spends one, whatever you harvest banks into that town's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Between visits you spend the stockpile on growth.

The long arc is a ladder of seven towns, all required. You found each by caravan, build it up, beat its signature task, and roll on — towns getting bigger, their board-stack taller, their hazards meaner. Workers, coins, and your global tool kit travel with you; each town's resource stockpile is its own. The whole game is that ladder, ending at the Old Capital.

The board & the chain

The board is a 6×6 grid of tiles from a few families. You play by dragging through a run of touching, same-family tiles — three or more, in any of 8 directions, no reuse. Release and the chain resolves; the board collapses and refills; one turn is spent.

Every chain does two things at once — the core decision of the game:
  • It harvests — a chain of N tiles banks floor(N ÷ divisor) of that resource. Longer chain → more goods.
  • It merges — a long-enough chain promotes a tile up its ladder (grass→bird, grain→vegetable…). Longer chain → a better board.
You can't max both at once. Reading the board for the best chain is the puzzle.

Tiers & divisors. Each family climbs a tier ladder; higher tiers yield more. The divisor sets how generous a family is — small for staples (grass), big for premium goods (flowers, gems, gold). Refining turns raw harvest into the goods the game prices in: Mill→flour, Bakery→bread, Kitchen→supplies, Forge→bars & tools.

Founding by caravan

You leave every town the same way: by building a caravan and rolling it out to found the next site. Town 1's entire win condition is building that first caravan; thereafter each town's signature task unlocks the caravan onward. Founding spends a (rising) coin cost for the journey — and, for later towns, the caravan can carry a starter basket of supplies. Home is where you start; every town after is caravan-founded.

today a founding cost already exists (~300 × 1.7ⁿ coins, escalating per town) and the map tracks visited/adjacent nodes. design wrap that in the caravan as one legible act, gated on the previous town's win.

Workers — the multiplier (and the main cost)

You hire workers with coins + resources. Each hire permanently lowers production cost (fewer tiles per unit) or adds an ability. Workers are global — they follow you to every town — so they're the engine that makes the flywheel accelerate across the whole run.

Worker hiring is also the game's main coin + resource sink, and the design wants it to bite harder: scale both the coin and the material cost up, and steeper.

Tools & the building → tool unlock

Tools are board powers you craft and spend — clear a tile, shuffle, drop a bomb, reveal ore, clear a hazard. Your tool inventory is global today (one kit across every town).

The design ties tools to buildings: building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted — and some buildings generate a tool on a timer instead. So your town layout literally decides your kit. today the seed exists — the Town Hall maps buildings to tools (Mill→clear, Workshop→basic, Forge→rare, Granary→shuffle, Sawmill→axe, Powder Store→bomb), Powder Store actually grants its tool, and ~30 tools have craft recipes. design make "build unlocks the recipe (or the timed grant)" the universal rule. Expeditions, hazards & provisioning

The stacked boards aren't free. Expedition boards (mine, fishery) make you pack what an earlier board produced to buy turns: spend farm food (and, for the fishery, mine metal) to depart, play till it runs out, and keep everything you gathered (soft fail, never a wipe). That exchange rate — how much farming funds one descent — is the single biggest pacing dial.

Hazards arrive gradually as the towns climb: none in the first two, then mine hazards (cave-ins, gas, moles), tides, rats, and combinations. They eat tiles or block you and force a response — urgency the gentle farm never had.

Timed generation. design some buildings generate a small, capped batch every few real-time hours — supplies or a tool — a gentle idle faucet that helps you provision between sessions without grinding. today the closest is the 8-hour Town-Hall "provisions" (tools delivered as a board crate) and the season-end Powder-Store bomb grant; the design generalises that to an "every X hours" building ability.

The economy: value from faucets to sinks

The whole game is a tank. Faucets pour value in (mostly the board, plus a ring of always-on systems). Sinks drain it out (everything you spend to grow or to open the next town). The design lives in the loop: the biggest sinks pour back into the faucets, so spending on growth grows production.

What coins are for

Coins are the soft, global currency. The risk today is that they pour in (board, daily, quests, achievements, bosses) but have shallow sinks, so they pile up. The design gives them real, non-locking outlets:

Hire workers

The main, scaling sink (coins + resources). Bites harder as we steepen the curve.

Found the next town

The caravan journey cost, rising each time you roll out.

Upgrade town tiers

Coins + goods per rung. Good because it's optional progression, not a gate on earning.

The Market

Spend coins to buy resources or tools you're short on. The always-available outlet that keeps surplus coins meaningful — the cleanest answer to "what are coins for?"

Conveniences

Reroll a quest, restock supplies, expand a cap — small optional spends.

On "coins to start a farm round" — careful. The farm is your main coin source, so charging coins to play it risks a soft-lock / death-spiral: bottom out, can't pay to play, can't play to earn. If you want a per-play stake, put it on expeditions (mine/fishery), where the free farm is always the fallback to recover — or guarantee a free farm fallback. Gating the farm itself behind coins is the one place "pay to play" becomes game-locking. Decided (per review): the farm stays free — no coin cost to start a round. Coins matter through the Market + town tiers + workers + caravan founding instead.

The shape of the whole thing

The economy is lopsided — on purpose. Faucets are abundant (the board plus the meta ring). Sinks are resource-gated — buildings want big baskets, worker hires want coins and escalating resources (more so once scaled). Difficulty lives in the sinks; the worker flywheel slowly dissolves the early grind. Adding the Market and steeper worker costs is how we keep both coins and resources meaningful all the way up.

The seven-town path

Seven towns, all required, each founded by caravan. Every town adds at most one new board to the stack (Farm → Mine → Fish), one crux (the thing it teaches), and one win task — a signature "big task" that needn't be a boss. Towns get bigger and harder; hazards arrive only after the basics are taught.

Sizes are the design target (T1 deliberately tiny, the finale largest). Authored maps exist today at home 3→20, quarry 2→12, mirefen 3→15 plots — these can be re-sized / re-skinned to fit the ladder.

Town 1 Hearthwood Vale — the farm specified to numbers

Boards
Farm only — the free base board.
Crux
The tutorial hub. Teaches every verb — chain, refine, fill orders, tier up, hire a worker — with no pressure. Stays your home forever.
Hook
None by design: the gentle teacher. Seasons turn the board over; nothing attacks you.
Size
~6 plots (deliberately tiny — re-sized down from the authored 20-plot home map). A handful of buildings, no sprawl.
Win task
Build the Caravan. Gather the goods to assemble the wagon — and the moment it's built, you found Town 2. The win condition is the bridge to the next town.
  1. First chains. Drag 3+ touching tiles; each chain banks crops and, if long, merges a tile up a tier. One of ~10 turns.
  2. Refine. Grain → Mill → flour → Bakery → bread, the staple everything is priced in. Granary stores it; the Kitchen turns grain into supplies for later.
  3. Earn & shape. Fill villager orders for coins; hire your first worker (cheaper forever, travels with you). Quests/achievements tick in the background.
  4. Build the caravan. Stockpile the caravan's basket, assemble it — the "you've learned it" moment — and roll out to found Two Rivers.

Town 2 Two Rivers — the connection specified to numbers

Boards
Farm Mine — the first stacked town, one shared stockpile.
Crux
The whole point: the farm feeds the mine. A calm, generic valley where two boards first share a stockpile — farm food buys mine expeditions, the mine pays out ore, ore refines into metal. No distractions; just learn the connection.
Hook
None — no hazards. Deliberately the safe town where you internalise the supply chain before anything threatens it.
Size
~10 plots.
Win task
Fill a grand order — a combined shipment that needs both farm goods (bread) and mine metal (iron), proving you can run the two boards together. Builds the caravan onward.
  1. Found & bootstrap. Caravan to the valley; workers/tools came with you, the stockpile starts empty.
  2. Run the farm board. Free turns: chain grain, build the Kitchen, convert grain → supplies.
  3. Open the mine. Spend supplies to depart an expedition; chain stone/iron; run out of food and you keep everything mined. No hazards yet — just the rhythm.
  4. Refine & connect. Forge ore → metal; see the farm refill the pantry while the mine empties it. Hire mine workers (global).
  5. Fill the grand order. Assemble the bread + iron shipment, complete it, build the caravan, roll on.

Why the win tasks vary. Caravan-build (T1), grand order (T2), boss (T3), regatta (T4), great building (T5), sea boss/shipment (T6), monument (T7) — so "completing a town" never feels rote. today completion is "build enough + resolve the town's keeper → hearth token", and the finale opens on 3 biome tokens. design reframe the completion moment as each town's signature task, and gate the finale on all six towns (since they're all required), not just three tokens.

Buildings unlock tools — globally

A town's plots are scarce, and what you build on them decides your kit: building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted for the rest of the run — and a few buildings generate a tool/supply on a timer instead. Plots are few in Town 1 and grow with each town, so early on you're choosing which tools to bring online.

today tools are already a global inventory; ~30 tools have craft recipes; the Town-Hall "provisions" already map buildings to tools (Mill→clear, Workshop→basic, Forge→rare, Granary→shuffle, Sawmill→axe, Powder Store→bomb); and Powder Store fully grants its tool at season-end. design make "build unlocks the recipe (or the timed grant)" the universal rule. Slots < buildings — choose your abilities. The recommendation: a town's slots are fewer than the buildings on offer. After the core chain (the buildings the boards literally need — Mill/Bakery/Kitchen, Forge/Workshop, Harbor Dock) you have only a few discretionary slots and must pick which ability / yield / tool buildings to run. That choice — which tools to bring online, which yields to boost — is the early game's main strategy, and it's why Town 1 is tiny. Because building a building unlocks its tool globally and permanently, the choice is about pacing and per-town board identity, not permanent loss — you'll unlock most tools eventually, but when and where is yours. design open question: should slots instead equal the building count (build everything, no choice)? The systems that wrap the loop

Around the board sits a ring of always-on systems. None is the game, but each shapes how it feels — pacing a session, rewarding a return, sequencing the story, compounding your power. What feeds each, and what it feeds back:

Workers — the multiplier

Coins + (scaling) resources in; permanently cheaper production + abilities out. Global. The accelerator of the flywheel and the biggest sink — see Mechanics for the cost direction.

Story — the sequencer

Thresholds (a total, a building, a win task) in; the next town, boss summons, rare resources out. Decides when each part of the game opens.

Achievements

Passive counters in; mostly coins, a few special tools out. One-way faucet rewarding mastery & breadth.

Quests

A handful of seasonal goals in; coins + XP out. Gives a session a target beyond "play till turns end."

Daily login

Showing up in; a streak of coins + tools out. Pure retention.

Civic tax & timed grants

Time + how much you've built in; coins + tool provisions (a board crate) out, plus the every-X-hours building grants. The game's idle-ish layer.

Tools

Unlocked by buildings, fed by daily/civic/grants; spent as board powers. Global. The shared "spend" tying side-faucets to play.

Bosses → runes → portal

Beating a boss pays coins + a rune; runes are meant to fuel portal summons — still being wired (see Gaps). Today runes mostly accumulate.

One pattern to notice: most side systems pay out in coins — fine now that the Market gives coins a real home (see Economy). Without it, they'd just inflate.
