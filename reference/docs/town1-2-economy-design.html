What the game is

puzzleDrag2 is a cozy match-merge farming & settlement game. On a small board you drag chains of matching tiles; each chain harvests resources and merges tiles into better ones. You spend what you grow to build up a settlement — and once a settlement is thriving, you send a wagon over the ridge to found the next one. Each new place is a fresh board with its own twist (a quarry to mine, a richer farm overrun by rats), but always the same heart: drag, harvest, grow, expand.

The fantasy. You arrive at a cold hearth in a forgotten vale and someone hands you the tongs. You re-light the farm one chain at a time, grow a hamlet into a city, then fund a wagon to discover what lies beyond — carrying your people and your know-how forward as the kingdom wakes up around you.

The core loop

Everything in the game is one compounding loop. You play the board to harvest; you spend the harvest on growth; growth makes the board produce more; so the next round of play is bigger. That flywheel is the whole game — every system in this doc either feeds it or is fed by it.

The same four beats repeat in every town, at every scale — from your first chain to founding your fifth settlement.

The shape of a session & the long arc

A session is one visit to a board. You get a small budget of turns (~10); each chain you drag spends one. Whatever you harvest banks into that settlement's stockpile, and the visit ends when turns run out. Between visits you spend the stockpile on growth.

The long arc is a chain of settlements. The first one teaches the whole loop and stays your permanent home; finishing it opens the next; finishing that opens the one after. Your workers travel with you and your mastery carries forward, but each place keeps its own stockpile — so every new town is a fresh start on resources with a head start on skill. The game is that ladder of settlements, each a new variation on the one loop.

The board & the chain

The board is a 6×6 grid of tiles drawn from a few families (grass, grain, trees, birds…). You play by dragging through a run of touching, same-family tiles — three or more, in any of the 8 directions, never reusing a tile. Release, and the chain resolves: the tiles you traced clear, the board collapses and refills, and that's one turn spent.

Every chain does two things at once — and that tension is the core decision of the game:
  • It harvests. A chain of N tiles banks floor(N ÷ divisor) units of that family's resource. Longer chain → more goods.
  • It merges. A long-enough chain also promotes a tile up its ladder (grass→bird, grain→vegetable…), permanently improving the board's mix. Longer chain → more / better tiles.
You can't max both — a chain spent earning isn't spent improving. Reading the board for the best chain is the puzzle.

Tiles, tiers & resources

Each tile family climbs a tier ladder. Higher-tier tiles are worth more per chain, so a board that's been merged-up produces far more than a fresh one. The divisor (tiles needed per unit of resource) is the dial that sets how generous a family is: cheap staples like grass have a small divisor (lots of yield); premium goods like flowers, gems and gold have a big one (rare, precious). That spread is what keeps a bushel of hay common and a cut gem special.

Raw tiles aren't the end of the line — they feed refining.

Refining: raw → valuable

Buildings turn raw harvest into the goods the rest of the game actually prices in:

  • Mill grain → flour, Bakery flour → bread — bread is the staple everything downstream is measured against.
  • Kitchen grain → supplies — the ration that pays for mine expeditions.
  • Forge / Workshop ore → bars & tools — what you build the next tier out of.

Refining is the bridge between "stuff I chained" and "stuff that unlocks the next thing." A refined good is usually the input to a sink (a building cost, an expedition ration), which is why the refining chain is the spine of progression.

The dials you spend on: workers, tools, buildings, tiers

Workers

Hired with coins + resources. Each hire permanently lowers the cost to produce (fewer tiles per unit) or adds an ability (promote a category, cut a recipe's cost, bonus coins). Workers are global — they follow you to every settlement. This is the main thing that makes the flywheel spin faster over time.

Buildings

Built from resources. Each one grants an ability or unlocks a recipe / a new tile family on the board. Buildings are what add new faucets and sinks to a town (a Coop adds birds; a Kitchen adds the supplies → expedition loop).

Town tiers

Outpost → Hamlet → Village → City. You spend resources to climb; each rung adds plots (room for more buildings) and unlocks the next tier of buildings. Tiers never change your turn budget or yield directly — they grow the room to grow.

Tools

Consumable board powers — clear a tile, shuffle, drop a bomb, swing an axe. You earn them from many systems and spend them to bail out a bad board or set up a huge chain. Tools are global and persist between sessions.

Expeditions & hazards: when the board pushes back

Not every board is free to play. Expedition boards (the mine, later the harbor) make you pack food to buy turns: spend rations to depart, play until they run out, and keep everything you gathered even if you leave early (a soft fail, never a wipe). The richer the ration, the more turns it buys — and that food→turns rate is the single biggest pacing dial in the game, because it sets how much farming funds one trip down the mine.

Some boards also spawn hazards — cave-ins, gas vents and moles in the mine; rats on a rich farm — that eat tiles or block you and demand you respond. Hazards are how a board creates urgency the gentle starter farm never does.

The through-line. Board → harvest → refine → spend on a dial → the board gets better or a new board opens. Hold that picture; the next tab shows every place value enters and leaves that flow.

The economy is value flowing from faucets to sinks

Think of the whole game as a tank. Faucets pour value in (mostly the board, plus a ring of always-on systems). Sinks drain it out (everything you spend on to grow or to open the next place). The art of the design is the loop: the most important sinks pour straight back into the faucets, so spending on growth makes production grow — the flywheel from the first tab, now drawn in full.

The three loops that matter

1 · Reinvestment (the flywheel)

Board → resources → tile upgrades + worker hires → the board yields more → repeat. This is the engine of the whole game; it compounds and never stops.

2 · Growth

Resources → buildings + town tiers → more plots, new recipes, new tile families → which add new faucets and sinks. This is how a town gets wider, not just faster.

3 · The expedition spine

Farm food → expedition turns → ore → refine → build → more capacity. This is the supply-chain heart of the mine town: one board literally pays to run another.

The shape of the whole thing (and its tension)

The economy is lopsided — on purpose, but it needs watching. Faucets are abundant (the board, plus daily / civic / quests / achievements / bosses all dripping value). Sinks are heavily resource-gated — buildings want big resource baskets, and worker hires want coins and escalating resources. So the real difficulty lives in the sinks, and the game is front-loaded on early grind that the worker flywheel slowly dissolves.

One consequence is worth flagging up front, because it shapes a lot of the open questions in the last tab: coins are an easy faucet with thin sinks. Tiers and most buildings are paid in resources, the Market doesn't really buy anything yet, so once you're past founding costs, coins pile up with little to do. "What are coins for?" is the single biggest balance question this map raises — and it's a design question, not a tuning one.

The towns are one arc

The committed slice is three settlements that teach the loop, then complicate it, then turn it back on the player. Each is the same core loop wearing a different costume and carrying a different lesson.

What carries vs. what's siloed

The single rule that makes the arc feel coherent: you carry forward, your goods don't.

Because workers are global but stockpiles aren't, arriving in a new town feels like "I'm skilled but starting over" — the right kind of fresh start. It also means a new town's early economy leans on the old town's surplus only through what you can carry as coins, not bulk goods.

Town 1 — the farm (learn the loop)

Role: tutorial and permanent hub. It's the grain → bread farm, and it teaches every verb in the game without any of the pressure later towns add. It never fully retires — it's home.

  1. First chains. Drag through 3+ touching matching tiles. Each chain banks some of that crop and, if it's long enough, merges a tile up a tier. That's one of your ~10 turns.
  2. Refine. Send grain to the Mill → flour, flour to the Bakery → bread. Bread is the staple everything downstream is priced in.
  3. Earn on the side. Fill villager orders for coins, and watch quests and achievements tick quietly as you play (your first chain pays out on its own).
  4. Shape the board. Raise buildings that add tile families and hire your first worker (cheaper production, and it follows you forever).
  5. Grow the town. Spend farm goods to climb Outpost → Hamlet → Village → City. Each rung adds plots and buildings — at Village the Kitchen appears, turning grain into the supplies that will fund mining.
  6. Open the next place. Reach City, then fund and raise the wagon — the one clear "you're ready" moment — and roll out to discover Town 2.
Completion feel. Town 1 is "done" the moment you can raise the wagon at City — proof you've learned chain → refine → orders → tier-up → worker.

Town 2 — the combination (the supply chain made literal)

Role: the idea of the whole game in one place. Town 2 is a single settlement with two boards sharing one stockpile — a farm board that runs on normal turns (your food engine) and a mine board that runs as paid expeditions. The farm grows food; the food buys descents into the mine; the mine pays out ore. One board literally funds the other.

  1. Arrive & bootstrap. The wagon (gated until home was a City) crests the ridge onto a shattered pit. Found the camp with coins; your workers and tools came with you, but the stockpile starts empty.
  2. Run the farm board. Free, repeatable turns. Chain grain, build the Kitchen, convert grain → supplies.
  3. Pack a basket & descend. Spend supplies to depart a mine expedition. Each chain spends a turn — stone, iron, deeper goods — while cave-ins, gas and moles press you to chain well. Run out of food and the run ends, but you keep everything you mined.
  4. Build the mine up. Spend ore to climb the mine's tier ladder, unlocking the Forge and metal tools; hire mine workers (global). The farm refills the pantry; the mine empties it.
  5. Loop the combination. Farm to refill → descend to mine → refine → grow. Two economies, one stockpile, each feeding the other — this is Town 2.
  6. Close it out. Build the mine up and beat the ★ capstone boss — the dramatic proof of farm + mine mastery.
The payoff is the open question. Finishing a town should visibly ease everything after it — the natural idea is a slow passive trickle of mine goods into your home. But an idle layer was actually built and later removed from the game, so "what's the right ongoing reward for finishing a place?" is unresolved. Flagged in Gaps.

Town 3 — the richer farm & the rats (the loop turns on you)

Role: the slice's twist. It comes back to the farm, but this time abundance is the threat: as your barns fill, rats appear and eat adjacent crops each turn. The lesson is hazard management on the farm side — the mirror of the mine's danger.

Play-through (sketch). You settle a lusher hold and your stockpile climbs — which draws rats. You learn to fight them: chain rats directly to clear them, build the Ratcatcher (free moves on rats) and the Master Ratcatcher (clear rats together with grass in one chain). Underneath, a deeper crop economy and the town's own specialization open up. You complete it by taming the rats while climbing the richer-farm ladder.

The systems that wrap the loop

Around the core board loop sits a ring of always-on systems. None of them is the game, but each one shapes how it feels — pacing a session, rewarding a return, sequencing the story, or compounding your power. Here's what feeds each one and what it feeds back.

Workers — the multiplier

Fed by: coins + resources (escalating per hire). Feeds back: permanently cheaper production, plus abilities — promote a category, cut a recipe's cost, bonus coins. There are ~16 production-line workers, ~7 promoters, and a few coin/rune specialists. This is the system that makes the flywheel accelerate; it's both the biggest sink and the biggest payoff.

Story — the sequencer

Fed by: reaching thresholds — a resource total, a building, a boss, a keeper choice. Feeds back: unlocks the next zone, summons bosses, reveals rare resources. Story is the spine that decides when each part of the game opens; the rest of the loop is the verb, story is the order.

Achievements

Fed by: passive counters (chains made, orders filled, bosses beaten, families harvested). Feeds back: mostly small coin payouts, a few special tools. A one-way faucet that quietly rewards mastery and breadth.

Quests

Fed by: a deterministic set of goals each season (collect / craft / order / long chain). Feeds back: coins + XP on claim. Their job is to give a session a target beyond "play until turns run out."

Daily login

Fed by: showing up. Feeds back: a streak ladder of coins and tools. Pure retention; orthogonal to the board.

Civic tax (Town Hall)

Fed by: time + how much you've built (per settlement, per building). Feeds back: coins and tool "provisions" — which land as a crate on the board you must match to claim. The closest thing to an idle layer that's currently live.

Tools

Fed by: almost everything (daily, achievements, civic, building grants, quests). Feeds back: board powers you spend to rescue or supercharge a board. The shared "spend" pool that ties the side-faucets to moment-to-moment play.

Bosses → runes → portal

Fed by: beating the seasonal boss (coins + a rune + XP). Feeds back: runes are meant to fuel portal summons — but that payoff is still being wired (see Gaps). Today runes mostly accumulate.

One pattern to notice: most of these side systems pay out in coins. That's fine only if coins have somewhere meaningful to go — which, per the economy tab, they mostly don't yet. The meta ring and the "what are coins for?" question are the same problem seen from two sides.

What this unlocks

With the picture drawn, the next moves are concrete and in order:

  1. Resolve the design questions (the three question rows) — these change what the game is, so they come first.
  2. Close the seams (the half-wired rows) — finish or cut, so the map matches the code.
  3. Then balance. Only once value reliably flows faucet → pool → sink → back does tuning the numbers (divisors, costs, the food→turns rate, the trickle) mean anything.
The one-sentence summary. puzzleDrag2 is a compounding loop — drag to harvest, spend to grow, grow to harvest more — wrapped in a ring of side systems and stretched across a ladder of towns; the work now is making every faucet and sink connect cleanly before we tune how hard each one runs.
