The Player Journey

The ideal user journey through Hearthwood Vale, end to end — which systems the player meets, when each one enters, the want that earns it, and the pacing target for each phase. The connective tissue the game's many built systems are still missing.

Overview

puzzleDrag2 has most of its systems built — the drag-chain board, refining, orders, the market, per-zone tier ladders, the mine, fishing, bosses, story beats. What it does not have is the journey that links them: the order they enter, the want that earns each one, and the pace at which they arrive. This doc defines that journey.

TL;DR. The intended shape is already locked — Town 1 (farm) → expedition → Town 2 (mine + the combination) → branch into open exploration → the Long Return. The spine is even wired in data. But it can't execute (a circular softlock walls the first step) and its pacing is unmodeled. This artifact captures the full-arc journey, the feature-introduction schedule, and the design calls that make it run.

"Balancing the journey" is two jobs, in order

1 · Structural spine

Does the journey connect — in the right order, one new idea at a time, with no softlock? Modelled by the code-derived progression oracle. RED today — a fresh save is hard-walled at the first rung.

2 · Pacing curve

How long does each phase take versus the session-shape target (~2–3 short runs/day, steady ~1–2 weeks to mid-game)? Modelled by the campaign harness. un-instrumented across zones — the sim only plays the farm board today.

You can't pace a journey that doesn't connect, so the structural fix comes first, then the pacing curve, then the numbers. That ordering is the plan (see Pacing & Next).

The principle that answers "when do we add a feature?"

Introduce a system the phase after the player feels the want for it — and never before its inputs are producible.

The softlock is exactly this rule violated: the game demands bread to grow the town before it has taught or enabled bread. Read the Feature Schedule as this principle applied system by system. Two corollaries shape everything below:

  • Soft efficiency gates, not hard possession gates, for cross-system dependencies. A rung that requires a good can deadlock if that good isn't producible yet. A dependency where the raw input works but the crafted one works better can never deadlock. (This is the "raw food buys mine turns, supplies buy more" model — generalised.)
  • One forcing function per phase. Each phase should answer "what do I do next?" with one obvious thing — the expedition is the model. Compress choice early (linear farm→mine), expand it late (branch).

Phase by phase

Phase A

The Drag — the core verb, alone

The first run teaches one thing: drag through 3+ adjacent matching tiles → a resource is credited and coins drop. Nothing else is on screen — no map, no town, no crafting. The whole game is one satisfying verb before it is anything else.

New
board · drag-chain · turn budget · chain→resource→coins · the season backdrop
Want
none yet — this creates the want (coins, a fuller board)
Gate out
complete a run (close the season)
Pacing
run 1, first ~10 minutes
Phase B

Farm → Town — the core loop forms

Coins from Phase A buy a reason to build. The player meets the town, places buildings, learns the refining chain (grain → flour at the Mill → bread at the Bakery), fills orders and trades at the market, and performs the first tier-ups (Outpost → Hamlet → Village → City; plots 3 → 6 → 12 → 20). The closed loop: chain grain → bake bread → spend farm goods to tier up → unlock more plots & buildings → chain more.

New
inventory · town · buildings · refine→bread · orders · market · 3 tier-ups
Want
"more plots and building types" — created by scarce plots at Outpost
Gate out
reach City, which raises the wagon for the expedition (one forcing function)
Pacing
runs ~2–22; a visible "the town grew" beat every ~3–7 runs
This is where the journey breaks today. The first rung (Hamlet) demands bread; bread needs the Bakery; the Bakery costs block (a mine good) and only unlocks at Hamlet — the rung it gates. Town 1 must be made self-contained on farm goods (Decision 2). Until then, Phase B is unreachable past its first step.
Phase C

The Combination — farm + mine, together

The expedition opens Town 2, the mine. Its lesson is the combination: mine turns are bought with food. You can spend raw farm goods directly to buy turns, or — better — convert them at the Kitchen into supplies for more turns per good. Stone chains yield block, ore chains yield iron_bar; the Workshop/Forge turn metal into better tools; hazards (cave-ins, gas, moles) add urgency the farm never had. The phase — and the introductory loop — closes on a capstone boss.

New
expedition · mine board · food→turns (soft gradient) · metal tools · hazards · ★ boss
Want
"my farm is mature — give it a second use" — created by a maxed Town 1
Gate out
beat the capstone boss; the map opens. This is mid-game.
Pacing
runs ~22–35 (days 8–14) — the steady ~1–2 week target lands here
Why "raw also works" matters. Because raw food always buys at least some mine turns, the mine can never softlock on "I didn't craft supplies." Crafting is the optimization, not the key. This is the soft-efficiency-gate principle that retires the whole class of bug the bread softlock belongs to.
Phase D

The Frontier — branch & specialize

With farm + mine mastered, the map opens. The player founds zones in their own order — Town 3 (farm + rats) with its hazard-management lesson, the caves (rare gems), the harbor/Mirefen fishing biome, the orchard (luxury crops). Each is its own mini-Phase-B: found → climb its ladder → learn its specialization. Differentiation is by cost and difficulty, not a fixed sequence.

soft — accumulate toward the deep-end milestone (tokens)
Pacing
runs ~35–100; a zone's ladder in ~8–15 runs (the player is an expert now — faster than Town 1)
Phase E

The Long Return — the endgame chase  parked content

The completion arc. The deepest zones (the Black Forge, the Pit boss tier) yield hearth tokens; the three tokens open the Old Capital and end The Long Return. Per the direction doc this is deep-end gated — open most of the frontier early, but keep the finale behind a milestone so it stays earned.

Feature-introduction schedule

The direct answer to "when do we put in each system?". Every system enters the phase where the player has just developed an appetite for it and its inputs are producible — never earlier. Retention systems are the exception: they run from the start but gate nothing.

How to read this as a checklist. When proposing any new feature, ask the two questions: (1) which phase's want does it answer? (2) are its inputs producible by then? If it answers no existing want, it's too early; if its inputs aren't producible, it's a softlock waiting to happen.

Design calls (this discussion)

The decisions that shape the journey above, captured for the record. Five settled, one open.

settled 1 · Full-arc scope

The timeline runs A → endgame, not just the 3-town slice. Caveat carried forward: parked content (E) is paced most speculatively because it isn't built and may change.

settled 2 · Self-contained Town 1 (the softlock fix)

Re-cost the home tier ladder and the Bakery off all mine goods, and unlock the Bakery at Outpost. The entire farm → bread → tier-up loop must close on farm goods alone. Mine goods are first demanded in Town 2. This makes Town 1 a clean farm tutorial and flips the progression oracle from RED to green.

settled 3 · Soft efficiency gates for cross-system dependencies

Settlement-tier costs stay hard accumulation gates (the spine should make you save up — that's the pacing). But every cross-system dependency is a soft gradient: the raw input works, the crafted one works better. Structurally retires the softlock class of bug. First instance: food→mine-turns.

settled 4 · The combination is in v1, soft

Town 2 teaches food→mine-turns as its core lesson, but raw farm goods buy turns directly (crafted supplies just buy more). The "aha" without a hard stop.

settled 5 · Frontier opens after Town 2, deep-end gated

On beating the capstone boss the branch zones become foundable in any order, differentiated by cost/difficulty. The deepest content (forge, pit, capital) stays behind a later milestone so the finale is earned.

open 6 · How crafting gets "real substance"

The one unresolved fork. The goal — crafting should feel like a process, not a silent instant button — is agreed. The lever is not.

OptionWhat it isTrade
Real-time timer your leancraft takes real seconds–minutes (non-monetized)runs on a different clock than the turn/season economy → idle-app arbitrage; nudges an active puzzler toward a check-in idle game; re-opens the monetization surface the "instant crafting / no-P2W" call deliberately closed
In-game-time gate recommendedcraft completes next run or ferments over a season (Stardew model)same anticipation + plan-ahead feel, but stays on your clock — no idle weirdness, no monetization surface

Recommendation: juice + a light in-game-time gate on the deeper refines only (basic refines stay instant so Town 1 stays snappy). Resolve when we set pacing.

The sequence from here

Agreed order of operations: render the journey → decide pacing → balance the numbers to match.

① Render the journey this doc

The full-arc spine, feature schedule, and design calls — the thing we review and sign off on.

② Decide pacing

Lock the per-phase run targets + the per-tier-up cost shape. Resolve the open crafting fork (Decision 6). Fix the softlock (Decision 2) so the oracle goes green and the journey actually executes.

③ Balance the numbers

Tune upgradeCost, recipe inputs, item values, and faucets to hit the targets — one lever family at a time, re-tested against the (extended) harness. Compress the ~10× family-value spread in the same pass.

Smallest first step whenever we move to code: Decision 2 (re-cost Town 1 off mine goods + unlock the Bakery at Outpost). It's the single change that turns the progression oracle from RED to green and makes the whole journey playable end-to-end — everything else is tuning on top of a spine that runs.
