Full design review · 2026-07

puzzleDrag2 — a complete design pass

Every view, layout, interaction, animation, and critical journey — read from the live code in src/ (not the reference/ docs), with the running game screenshotted across ~85 states at both a phone (390×844) and a desktop (1280×1024) viewport, and judged mobile-first through a frontend-design lens. Findings and concrete proposals, organized into the tabs at left.

The short version. This is a genuinely charming, deep game with a excellent signature interaction and a strong storybook art identity. The prior color/contrast pass landed well — the parchment UI now has a real contrast ladder. The work that's left is not more color. It is four things: (1) the game has no wide-screen layout — every DOM screen is an unadapted phone column stranded in empty parchment on desktop/web; (2) the town's world-overlays and its central Farm plot look unfinished next to the rich building art; (3) a class of legibility/polish papercuts (label truncation, redundant reward rows, grass "confetti" noise, dead desktop space); and (4) the reward/feedback and motion languages don't yet form one escalating ladder from the board out into the chrome. None require a rebuild — they're targeted, high-leverage fixes.

§How this was evaluated

  • Source of truth = the code. Assessments were formed from src/, the three Vite entries, and the running game — never from reference/ docs.
  • Screenshots of the real game. All ~85 visual scenarios (from src/visualTesting/matrix.ts) were rendered against the running dev server at a phone and a desktop viewport. Shots referenced below live in ./shots/.
  • Mobile-first. The primary target is a phone in portrait; density, tap targets, reach and truncation are judged there first. Desktop shots reveal full layouts.
  • Beyond color. A prior review already rebuilt the token contrast ladder, accent system, season accents and bundled the display font (see src/tokens.css). This pass deliberately pushes past that into layout, IA, motion/feel, per-view UX, and whether the player understands what is happening.
  • Method. Four subsystem maps (views/IA, critical journeys, design system, interaction/animation) grounded a fan-out of focused reviewers — one per surface and per cross-cutting lens — each citing the screenshot and the file:line behind every finding.

§What's genuinely strong — protect these

  • The chain-drag is a great signature interaction. The glowing path with node dots and halo, the star-burst upgrade previews, chain-length-scaled screen-shake + radial flash, escalating haptics and audio pitch, squash-stretch landing, and the BONUS→DOUBLE→TRIPLE→FRENZY combo panel all combine into a satisfying, legible harvest. The held-chain state (see the Board tab) reads beautifully. This is the heart of the game and it works.
  • The town & building art is rich and characterful. The painterly settlement — bakery, mill, portal, market stalls, houses, lamps, benches, river with a boat — genuinely feels like a place that grows across tiers.
  • The World Map is the best-realized screen. A cohesive parchment atlas with themed zone nodes, solid-vs-dashed roads, a clean detail panel and a real legend. It nails the storybook identity and it actually uses the widescreen space. It is the internal benchmark for every other screen.
  • Quest / tile / research cards are legible. Category tags, have/need progress bars, reward chips, and clear claim states make goals scannable.
  • The season system is ambitious and mostly reads. Distinct field gradients and per-season accents give each of the four seasons a felt identity.
