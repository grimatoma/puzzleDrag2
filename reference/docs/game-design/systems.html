The board & the chain

The board is a 6×6 grid of tiles from a few families. You play by dragging through a run of touching, same-family tiles — three or more, in any of 8 directions, no reuse. Release and the chain resolves; the board collapses and refills; one turn is spent.

Every chain does two things at once — the core decision of the game:
  • It harvests — a chain of N tiles banks floor(N ÷ divisor) of that material. Longer chain → more goods.
  • It merges — a long-enough chain promotes a tile up its ladder (grass→bird, grain→vegetable…). Longer chain → a better board.
You cannot max both at once. Reading the board for the best chain is the puzzle.

The two chain knobs

Every chain is read by two independent length gates, and that is where the long-game optimization lives:

Both start high and workers buy them down toward a floor (see Workers & tools). Cheap staples (grass, grain) hit the floor with a few hires; premium lines (flowers, gems, gold) start far higher and need many workers and time — so the accelerator is real but self-limiting. This is the depth knob behind the whole production curve, and the design keeps that depth rather than flattening it.

Researching new tiles

The board's vocabulary grows over the run: you research, buy, or earn new tile types, each with its own unique ability (a special yield, a chain effect, a hazard-clearer, a wildcard). They arrive from a research track (materials + time), the coin "unlock a tile" sink, or rewards (achievements, win-tasks, boss/festival drops). An unlock is permanent and global, so your kit of board pieces deepens alongside your buildings and tools — a third axis of identity. New tiles keep the 6×6 puzzle fresh as the towns stack and give coins and materials a meaningful, non-grindy, one-time sink.

Three currencies, deliberately unequal

The economy runs on three currencies that are not interchangeable — so your farming stays targeted, rather than earning a catch-all that buys anything. Materials and coins bridge only through a costly Market; Villagers come only from Houses.

Materials — the main currency

Per-town, board-grown (crops, ore, fish and their refined goods). They pay for buildings, win-tasks, expedition rations, and refining. They do not travel between towns, so each town's farming is purposeful — there is no super-currency that buys everything.

Coins — the cash layer

The money economy: worker wages, town-tier upgrades, tile unlocks, caravan founding, and the Market. Earned by selling surplus at the Market, plus quests, daily, civic and bosses. Because coins are global and portable, they are what funds opening a new town — so founding never depends on a town's own materials. Coins travel with you.

Villagers — the worker currency

Your population, produced by Houses. Spent (with coins and materials) to hire workers — see Workers & tools. A constrained, valuable resource, and the one optional-IAP lever (see Meta & money).

The Market — the coin ↔ material bridge

Sell surplus → coins (an outlet for overflow and lopsided builds) and buy materials → coins, priced deliberately high so coins never cheaply replace farming. The escape hatch that lets you run a specialized build and just buy what you chose to skip — a release valve, not a bypass.

Why the split. Materials are what you grow and spend to build; coins are the cash for wages, unlocks and flexibility; Villagers are population. Keeping them separate, with the Market bridge priced high, is what makes progression feel like an accomplishment rather than a wallet check.

The three loops that matter

1 · Reinvestment (flywheel)

Board → materials → tile upgrades + workers → the board yields more. The engine; it compounds. Steeper worker baskets plus the chain-knob floors keep it from trivialising itself.

2 · Growth

Materials → buildings (+ Houses for Villagers) → more plots, new tools, new families. How a town gets wider — and where the build-choice strategy lives (see Buildings & tools).

3 · Provisioning

Food → expedition turns → ore/fish → refine → build. In Deepdelve, timed depots and shipping replace the farm under scarcity (see Provisioning).

Workers, Villagers & hiring

Workers are the multiplier: each hire permanently buys down the chain knobs (see The two chain knobs) or adds an ability. They are global — they follow you to every town. Hiring is gated two ways:

Houses produce Villagers

Your population is a global pool, raised by building Houses. Your worker count is capped by how many Houses you have raised — population gates how many.

A hire costs Villager + coins + materials

Each hire spends a Villager (from your Houses), coins (wages) and materials, with the material cost scaling per profession: the more workers already in a line (miners, farmers…), the costlier the next. Over-stacking one profession self-limits — late workers are a real investment.

One home for this rule. Houses competing for building slots, and the rule for removing a House, are part of the build-choice strategy and live in Buildings & tools.

Tools & the building → tool unlock

Tools are board powers you craft and spend — clear a tile, shuffle, drop a bomb, find ore, clear a hazard. Your tool inventory is global: one kit across every town. The design ties tools to buildings — building a building unlocks its tool, globally, to be crafted, and a few buildings generate a tool on a timer. So your town layout decides your kit. The full building→tool map is in Buildings & tools.

Expeditions, hazards & timed supply

Expedition boards (mine, fishery) make you pack what an earlier board produced to buy turns: spend farm food (and, for the fishery, mine metal) to depart, play till it runs out, and keep everything you gathered (a soft fail, never a wipe). That exchange rate is the single biggest pacing dial in the game.

Hazards arrive gradually, matching the cozy-to-ramp difficulty: none in the first two towns, then cave-ins, gas and moles, then tides, rats, and combinations — they eat tiles or block you and force a response.

Timed generation is a helper, never a gate. Some buildings generate a small, capped batch every few hours — supplies or a tool — the free way to speed up between sessions. Nothing is time-locked: if you do not want to wait, craft the same tool now by spending materials. It is the spine of the farm-less Deepdelve (its Supply Depot) and a gentle boost everywhere else.
