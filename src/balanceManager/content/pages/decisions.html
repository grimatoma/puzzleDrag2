The confirmed, settled design decisions for Hearthwood Vale — the calls that are no longer up for debate and that shape everything on the Progression page. These were locked across the v2 World Bible and the master design doc. Scope: the Phase-1 plan (farm + mine + the first 1–3 towns). Dated to the v2 lock, 2026-06-01.

World & tone

  • Remember, not conquer. The central verb is rediscovery of a dormant network of vows. The kingdom is a portfolio of single-purpose settlements, not a conquered map.
  • Soft fail everywhere. No permanent setbacks. An expedition that runs out of food ends safely with everything gathered; a lost keeper fight is retried after preparation; a low-yield farm round is just low yield. The only cost is time and one-shot consumables.
  • No pay-to-win. Premium currency (gems) only ever skips real-time timers. It never buys puzzle advantage. No gambling, no loot boxes, no time-limited exclusives.

The three biomes

The game commits to exactly three settlement types, each a distinct board and economy:

  • Farm — pastoral resources, a year-structured board with season-driven spawn bias. Home zones like [[zones:home]], [[zones:meadow]], [[zones:orchard]].
  • Mine — earth resources ([[resources:block]], [[resources:iron_bar]], gems), an expedition board whose turns are bought with packed food. Zones like [[zones:quarry]], [[zones:caves]], [[zones:forge]].
  • Harbor — sea resources (fish, pearls), a tide-driven expedition board. The [[zones:harbor]] zone.

Why three: three axes (type, biome, specialization) give massive replayability from a small, legible set — a marsh farm specializing in forestry plays completely differently from a prairie farm specializing in crops, yet the minimum kingdom is just one of each. The economy is global; each settlement is independent for story and choice. The sea board is deferred as a progression slot so it can become "our own thing" later, rather than a generic third board now.

Farm rounds vs expedition rounds

  • Farm rounds are year-structured. A fixed turn budget split into four seasons (Spring/Summer/Autumn/Winter) that drive bonus mechanics and spawn bias — not hazards. (The standalone calendar-year season system was removed; only the in-session quartering and the seasonal visuals remain.)
  • Expedition rounds are supply-structured. Mine and Harbor rounds have no seasons. You bring food before the round; each food item has a turn value; you play until supplies run out. Longer runs mean more rolls at rare tiles.

The keeper / coexist mechanic

Each settlement's defining decision happens at its outer hearth. After clearing the keeper's challenge, you choose:

  • Coexist. The keeper stays; the hearth lights with their blessing. The biome keeps its wild properties. This is the spiritual path — boons change what appears on the board (spawn multipliers, tile transformations, threshold reductions). The board itself is the weapon. Earns Embers.
  • Drive Out. A boss fight; beat the keeper and claim the hearth. The biome becomes orderly. This is the industrial path — boons give more tools, charges, and yield. The toolbelt is the weapon. Earns Ingots.

The choice is final per settlement. To experience the other path, you found a new settlement of that type. The two currencies do not mix; there are no hybrid boons. Bosses such as [[bosses:frostmaw]] are encounters, not gates — you choose when to engage.

v2 note: in the current plan the keeper choice is paused for Phase 1 and will return as a qualitative fork rather than a multiplicative power boon, to avoid power creep.

Kingdom structure & carryover

  • Linear unlock + free revisit. Ruins light up roughly one main thread at a time; once unlocked, a settlement is freely revisitable to grind or craft. The first town is free; later ones cost an escalating resource fee and require completing at least one previous settlement.
  • Per-settlement tier ladders. Every ruin climbs the same Camp → City ladder; climbing it is the restoration. The top tier builds the wagon that wakes the next ruin — one obvious "what's next".
  • The carryover split. What is mechanical and progression-coded stays per-settlement (buildings, resources, tier, board); what is narrative knowledge or aesthetic carries forward kingdom-wide. Globally: coins, level/XP/Almanac, tools, knowledge, workers, runes. Per-zone: resources/inventory, buildings, town tier, board, hazards, biome.

Economy decisions

  • Resources stay per-zone. They are left behind on travel; there is no shared resource pool. Each ruin is a real rebuild, bootstrapped through its local Market with global coins. (This prevents flooding a new ruin with a mature kingdom's stockpile.)
  • Instant crafting. The real-time craft queue and gem-skip are removed — crafting resolves instantly. No freemium timers in the core loop.
  • Building-gated tile spawners. Beyond a settlement's two staple tiles, each new tile category spawns when its producing building is built (a Coop spawns birds, a Lumber Camp spawns trees), not at an opaque tier-up. The town you build reshapes the board you play.
  • Advancement = the wagon. Built at the top tier to unlock the next ruin. One clear forcing function, not a scattered set of lv gates.

Onboarding & endgame

  • The first settlement is the tutorial. The hearthkeeper [[npcs:wren]] walks the player through everything in the first 30–60 minutes. Multiple settlements are not mentioned until the first is complete; the Coexist / Drive-Out fork is the first major decision.
  • The Old Capital is the deferred finale. It unlocks once the player holds one Hearth-Token of each type. Its precise mechanics are intentionally left undefined until the rest of the game's identity is firmer.
  • Soft sandbox after the finale. No NG+, no carried benefits to fresh starts. The endgame is keeping the kingdom alive — annual Founder's Day, visiting descendants, optional hard-mode Quiet Years.

These decisions are settled. Open, simulator-driven questions (tuning curves, spawn weights, exact worker rosters) live on the Progression page, not here.
