1 · Vision & locked decisions

The fantasy: you arrive at a fallen kingdom and rebuild it one ruin at a time. Each settlement is a from-scratch restoration: clear the rubble, climb its town-tier ladder by chaining → refining → building, then raise the wagon that opens the next ruin. The whole arc culminates — much later — in restoring the Old Capital.

The problem this fixes (from v1): ~25 of 31 systems are visible from the first screen. The cure is PC2's: sequence, gate, hide, and connect — reveal one major system at a time along the rebuild ladder.

New session / cold start? Read §15 (current coded state) and §14 (where the data lives) first — they capture exactly what's implemented today and where every number is, so this doc is self-contained.

2 · Kingdom structure

Linear unlock + free revisit

Ruins light up roughly one main thread at a time (strong progressive disclosure). Once unlocked, a settlement is freely revisitable from the map — to grind its cheap resources or craft from its stockpile. The current cartography network (25+ nodes) is CHANGE trimmed/sequenced so only the next ruin (plus already-restored ones) is reachable; founding gates the rest.

Per-zone economy + the carryover split

This is what makes "leave resources behind" playable rather than punishing. Resources are stored per settlement; your meta-progression travels with you.

Carries globally (meta)Stays per-settlement
Coins — bootstrap a fresh ruin via its MarketResources / inventory (state.inventory → per-zone keyed)
Player level + XP + Almanac perksBuildings (built) — each town from scratch
Tools — craft at a rich old town, carry forwardTown tier / rebuild progress
Knowledge — researched tiles + known recipesBoard state, hazards, biome
Workers (hired once, help everywhere) + runes—
Biggest engineering change in the structure. Today state.inventory is a single global pool (see §15). Per-zone resources means re-keying inventory by settlement while keeping coins/tools/workers/level/knowledge global. The unlock registry and the Market (global coins → local resources) are what keep it from feeling like a soft-lock. This is the load-bearing refactor of Phase 1; the balance sim (§9) should explicitly test "can a fresh ruin bootstrap from coins alone."

The three bridges (PC2's connective tissue — Town 2+)

  • Farm food → mine turns. KEEP The expedition model exists (EXPEDITION_FOOD_TURNS, MIN_EXPEDITION_TURNS=3). Make it the hard gate at Town 2: a mine session's turns = packed food; out of food → session ends. CHANGE Replace the mine's lv2 unlock with a deterministic flag (kitchen_built && supplies ≥ 3) + 3 starter supplies for trip 1.
  • Mine metal → higher tiers + tools. CHANGE From Town 2, town-tier upgrades and the Workshop/Forge cost mine resources (block, iron_bar) — so the mine has a purpose beyond selling.
  • Better tools → both boards faster. KEEP Tools are global; crafting one at a metal-rich town speeds chaining everywhere.

3 · The town-tier ladder

Each ruin climbs the same rank ladder (Camp → City). A tier-up is a single town-center upgrade that costs escalating local resources and (a) unlocks the next batch of that settlement's content, (b) raises caps, (c) adds building plots. The top tier builds the wagon → next ruin. We map your existing 40 buildings onto these tiers instead of the scattered lv gates.

Boards are per-settlement — the table below is Town 1 (farm only). The same rank ladder gates every settlement, but the content it unlocks is drawn from that settlement's boards. Town 2 folds the mine board, Kitchen/supplies, the food→mine gate, metal buildings (Forge), the Miner + mine workers, and the capacity ladder into these same tiers — and its higher tiers then cost mine resources (block/iron_bar). See §7.

Player level is retained only for the slow Almanac perk drip, never to gate buildings.

6 · Balance math & ratios

The three core formulas (keep; surface clearly)

For a chain of length L on a tile with threshold T and coin-value v:

  • Upgrade tiles spawned = floor(L / T)
  • Resource credited = every T tiles → 1 inventory resource (fractional carryover in resourceProgress)
  • Coins = floor(L × v / 2)

So T does double duty (upgrade + resource) — both outcomes already fire in code, and we keep them unified.

The finding. The current mine spans 6→16 (flat); PC2 spans 10→450 (steep, roughly ×2–3 per tier). Flat means "dig deeper" is unrewarding — fatal once the mine is a co-equal pillar at Town 2. Adopt PC2's shape. Safe for construction (build costs are quantities, not value); it drives market income and the deep-mine fantasy. The sim (§9) must confirm the steeper ladder doesn't out-pace the farm and trivialize coins.

Worked economy (the inflection points)

  • 1 flour = 5 wheat tiles (T=5) → sells 8. A chain of 5 wheat (v=2) also pays floor(5×2/2)=5 coins.
  • 1 bread = 3 flour + 1 egg = 15 wheat + 6 bird tiles → sells 125. Raw 3 flour = 24c, so the Bakery ≈ 5× multiplier — the first big economic inflection (Town 1).
  • 1 supplies = 5 flour = 25 wheat tiles → 1 expedition turn (Town 2). 1 mine trip (3 turns) = 3 supplies = 15 flour = 75 wheat tiles. That's the throttle; the sim will say if 75 is too steep (candidate: supplies = flour×3 = 45 tiles).
Concrete balance bugs to fix (grounded in the configs — see §15):
  • BUG bread sells 125 in ITEMS but 4 in MARKET_PRICES — a stale market row. Make market sell = item value (bread = 125).
  • BUG Miner worker reduces category "wood" (workers/data.ts) — should target a mine category (e.g. mine_stone).
  • BUG First hire ≈ inert. Reductions fold as amount × hired / maxCount with maxCount:10, so hire #1 = 0.1 tiles. Fix: lower maxCount to ~5, or floor per-hire so hire #1 = a clean −1.
  • CHANGE Trim FARM_TILE_POOL — today it's 10 categories from the first board (hay×3 + 9 singles). Stage-0 pool = hay×4, wheat×2; add a category as its tier unlocks.
  • CHANGE Drop block from early farm buildings (Bakery's 10 block) — it's a mine resource; Town 1 has no mine.
  • CHANGE Re-gate buildings from lv to tier/flag (§3).

8 · The long-term sink — capacity ladder

The Old Capital is the endgame sink (deferred with the meta). The mid-game sink — active from Town 2 (the first mine) — is a rising capacity ladder, a clean PC2-Castle analog that answers "what do I keep improving?"

Because workers and tools are global (decision #10), the capacity ladder is a global meta-building — it travels with you and is the thing always worth grinding the mine for. It is capacity, not raw power, so it doesn't compound into the boon-creep problem.

11 · The deferred layer — net-new reward homes

The principle that unblocks the meta. "What's the reward for keepers/bosses, when global rewards are already strong?" is a supply problem: the power economy (workers, free turns, tools, tiers, capacity ladder) is saturated, so any new power reward is redundant or game-breaking. The fix is to add new demand, not more supply — build a net-new system the player wants to feed, and the meta rewards have a home that's impossible to get elsewhere. Don't run a parallel multiplier economy.
  • The "third pillar" slot (where PC2 puts its sea puzzle) is reserved for our own thing — most likely a collection / curation pillar. The Coexist/Drive-Out choice then becomes which collection you build: a living menagerie of spared spirits vs a trophy hall of bested ones. Bosses each drop a unique specimen/trophy. The reward is prestige & completion — non-breaking by construction.
  • Keepers return as the settlement capstone (surface only after town 1) → a qualitative board-rule fork + the Hearth-Token (pure progression).
  • Old Capital = the endgame restoration sink; Charter "reads your record" of choices.
  • Progression innovation (parked seeds): settlements that buff each other once both restored; a kingdom map that visibly reconnects; a "pick which ruin next" menu. Revisit after towns 1–3 play.
