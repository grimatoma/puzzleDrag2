Kingdom from the Ruins — Progression & Tuning Plan

A rebuilt progression plan and work roadmap, co-designed against the coded mechanics and the Puzzle Craft 2 reference. Phase 1 target: a complete, legible core loop across farm + mine + the first 1–3 towns. Narrative meta, bosses, and the sea board are paused and parked.

1 · Vision & locked decisions

The fantasy: you arrive at a fallen kingdom and rebuild it one ruin at a time. Each settlement is a from-scratch restoration: clear the rubble, climb its town-tier ladder by chaining → refining → building, then raise the wagon that opens the next ruin. The whole arc culminates — much later — in restoring the Old Capital.

The problem this fixes (from v1): ~25 of 31 systems are visible from the first screen. The cure is PC2's: sequence, gate, hide, and connect — reveal one major system at a time along the rebuild ladder.

New session / cold start? Read §15 (current coded state) and §14 (where the data lives) first — they capture exactly what's implemented today and where every number is, so this doc is self-contained.

2 · Kingdom structure

Linear unlock + free revisit

Ruins light up roughly one main thread at a time (strong progressive disclosure). Once unlocked, a settlement is freely revisitable from the map — to grind its cheap resources or craft from its stockpile. The current cartography network (25+ nodes) is CHANGE trimmed/sequenced so only the next ruin (plus already-restored ones) is reachable; founding gates the rest.

Per-zone economy + the carryover split

This is what makes "leave resources behind" playable rather than punishing. Resources are stored per settlement; your meta-progression travels with you.

Biggest engineering change in the structure. Today state.inventory is a single global pool (see §15). Per-zone resources means re-keying inventory by settlement while keeping coins/tools/workers/level/knowledge global. The unlock registry and the Market (global coins → local resources) are what keep it from feeling like a soft-lock. This is the load-bearing refactor of Phase 1; the balance sim (§9) should explicitly test "can a fresh ruin bootstrap from coins alone."

CHANGE Replace the mine's lv2 unlock with a deterministic flag (kitchen_built && supplies ≥ 3) + 3 starter supplies for trip 1.
  • Mine metal → higher tiers + tools. CHANGE From Town 2, town-tier upgrades and the Workshop/Forge cost mine resources (block, iron_bar) — so the mine has a purpose beyond selling.
  • Better tools → both boards faster. KEEP Tools are global; crafting one at a metal-rich town speeds chaining everywhere.

3 · The town-tier ladder

Each ruin climbs the same rank ladder (Camp → City). A tier-up is a single town-center upgrade that costs escalating local resources and (a) unlocks the next batch of that settlement's content, (b) raises caps, (c) adds building plots. The top tier builds the wagon → next ruin. We map your existing 40 buildings onto these tiers instead of the scattered lv gates.

Boards are per-settlement — the table below is Town 1 (farm only). The same rank ladder gates every settlement, but the content it unlocks is drawn from that settlement's boards. Town 2 folds the mine board, Kitchen/supplies, the food→mine gate, metal buildings (Forge), the Miner + mine workers, and the capacity ladder into these same tiers — and its higher tiers then cost mine resources (block/iron_bar). See §7.

Tier gates are legible ("upgrade your town center") and local (paid from this settlement's economy) — exactly the rebuild fantasy. Player level is retained only for the slow Almanac perk drip, never to gate buildings.

4 · Unlock schedule — what's available when & how

Everything is gated through one mechanism (the unlock registry, §10). Town 1 teaches the farm; Town 2 introduces the mine.

Building-gated tile spawners — how new tiles reach the board. Beyond each settlement's two staple tiles, a new tile category does not appear at tier-up — it appears when you build the structure that produces it. Raise the Coop and pheasants (eggs) start spawning; raise the Lumber Camp and oak (plank) appears; raise the Garden and carrots (soup) join the pool. The town you construct literally reshapes the board you play on. The forcing function: each tile's resource is consumed by a later building cost or the next settlement's found cost — plank gates the Mill/Bakery and every tier-up, eggs gate bread, the 12 soup is part of the City→Wagon cost — so every spawner is eventually required. But the player picks the order (Coop-first or Lumber-Camp-first, your call), so the board grows the way they choose. Town 2 follows the same rule — each ore tier (stone, iron, copper, coal, gem, gold) surfaces only when its matching mine structure is built. Spawner building names should match the live configs; where a category lacks a dedicated building today, add one (e.g. Coop, Garden) as part of the §10 backbone work.

6 · Balance math & ratios

The three core formulas (keep; surface clearly)

For a chain of length L on a tile with threshold T and coin-value v:

  • Upgrade tiles spawned = floor(L / T)
  • Resource credited = every T tiles → 1 inventory resource (fractional carryover in resourceProgress)
  • Coins = floor(L × v / 2)

So T does double duty (upgrade + resource) — both outcomes already fire in code, and we keep them unified. T is also the giant-chain throttle: because output scales as L/T, a huge early chain on a 2-type board feels great but pays little — see "Spawn weights & the early-board chain problem" below.

Spawn weights & the early-board chain problem

What the wiki actually specifies. Puzzle Craft 2 has no numeric spawn-rate table — spawn frequency is purely qualitative. Tiles are "Common / Uncommon / Rare," buildings make a species "appear" or "even more appear," and the load-bearing rule is one sentence: "Categories follow the farm production chain — each tier's long chains procure the next tier's species." So in PC2 a new tile category doesn't arrive from a rising spawn weight; it arrives because you chain the tier below it (long grass chains drop birds, long grain chains drop veg, …) and because you built the structure that enables it. That's the same building-gated model as decision #16 — the wiki backs it.

What our code already has — a real per-zone spawn-ratio table. The wiki has no numbers, but the game does: the farm board's authoritative spawner is pickByZoneSeasonDrops() (src/features/zones/data.ts), which samples from seasonDrops — a per-zone, per-season percentage table by category defined in src/features/cartography/data.ts. Two named tables exist today, and the fill path is: pending upgrades → seasonDrops (when the zone has data for the in-session season) → only then the weighted pool-array fallback (FARM/MINE/FISH_TILE_POOL + SEASON_POOL_MODS). So the doc should treat seasonDrops as the spawn-rate source of truth and the pool arrays as the fallback. The plan's job is to (a) trim these for the early game and (b) extend the model to the mine.

Gaps the plan must close: (1) every new zone should ship a named seasonDrops table reconciled to its theme rather than defaulting to empty; (2) the mine has no seasonDrops equivalent — it still spawns purely from MINE_TILE_POOL (stone×3, dirt×2, iron, copper, coal, gem); decision #16's building-gated ore reveal needs either a mine seasonDrops table or a building-conditioned pool filter; (3) early-game trim — at Camp the farm should expose only grass+grain, so the Stage-0 distribution is effectively {grass, grain} with the other categories at 0 until their spawner building is built (which raises that category's drop weight from 0).

How a new building adds its tile to the board — the mechanism already exists. Decision #16 ("build the Coop → birds start spawning") does not need a new system. There is a live ability channel for exactly this: pool_weight (id in src/config/abilities.ts; channel effectivePoolWeights; scope includes "building" alongside worker/tile). Any building can carry abilities: [{ id: "pool_weight", params: { target: <resourceKey>, amount: N } }]; at every board fill, aggregateAbilities sums it into effectivePoolWeights and applyPoolWeightAdds() (src/features/farm/poolMath.ts) pushes N extra copies of that tile into the spawn pool — so a category sitting at weight 0 only appears once its building stands. So the building→tile pipeline is: building built → its pool_weight ability folds in → that tile's spawn weight rises from 0. Two ways to wire the early-game gate, both using shipped machinery:
  • Pool-weight route (recommended, least new code): keep the Camp seasonDrops/pool at {grass, grain}; give the Coop/Garden/Lumber-Camp a pool_weight ability on their category's tile. Building the Coop adds birds; the board reshapes itself with zero bespoke logic. (Today no farm building ships a pool_weight ability — only workers/tiles use it — so this is the concrete §10 task: attach the ability to the spawner buildings.)
  • seasonDrops route (for the mine / themed zones): since the mine has no seasonDrops table, give it one whose ore categories start at 0 and are raised when the matching mine structure is built (a per-building override merged into the zone's drop table). Heavier lift than the pool-weight route; use only where you want season-varying ore rates too.
Interaction note: on the farm the seasonDrops sampler runs first and only falls through to the (pool-weight-adjusted) pool when the zone/season has no entry — so a building's pool_weight add is fully effective in empty4Seasons() zones and as the fallback elsewhere, but a fully-specified seasonDrops season will mask it. Keep early Camp seasons sparse (or empty) so building-driven weights actually show through.
The giant-chain problem you flagged — and why the turn limit doesn't solve it. A 6×6 board is 36 cells; the bottom-of-screen counter limits how many turns a session has (~10 farm / 5 mine), not how long a single chain can be. So if Stage 0 exposes only grass+grain (the §1 vision, expressed as a 2-category seasonDrops/pool), ~⅔ of the board is one colour and a single drag can sweep 15–20 tiles. The turn limit makes that worse, not better: fewer, bigger sweeps. Three levers already in the code keep it sane — note the turn counter is none of them:
  • Threshold T is the actual sink (already live — UPGRADE_THRESHOLDS + the CHAIN_COLLECTED wiring). A chain of length L yields only floor(L/T) upgrade tiles + L/T resources. A 20-hay sweep on T=6 = just 3 bundles + 3 upgrades + floor(20×1/2)=10 coins — big-feeling, small-paying. The giant chain is a fantasy moment, not an economy break, because of T. This is the answer to the question; §6-top now cross-links it.
  • Pool / drop-table width sets the natural chain length. 2 categories → trivially long chains; 3–4 → chains taper to a healthy 4–7 without any cap. The control already exists per-zone (seasonDrops) and per-pool (the arrays). Recommendation: open at 3–4 effective categories, not 2. Even before the Coop/Garden are built, the Camp board should carry grass + grain + 1–2 low-weight "texture" fillers (e.g. a non-refinable bush, or trees at a small weight) so the very first drag isn't a half-board sweep. As spawner buildings come online via pool_weight, the count climbs toward the full roster. The sim (§9) samples "a distribution of chain lengths per turn" — the new max natural chain on the Stage-0 distribution ≤ ~8 invariant guards this.
  • Min chain length (≥3 default; bosses raise it via boss.minChain — already coded) sets the floor, never the ceiling. We have no ceiling mechanic and deliberately want none — capping chain length would fight the core fantasy. The ceiling is economic (T), not mechanical.
Net: "limited turns + few tile types" does not produce a balance bug as long as T stays the throttle and the opening distribution is ≥3 effective colours. Decision needed: exact Stage-0 width (2 vs 3 categories) — flagged in §13.

Worked economy (the inflection points)

  • 1 flour = 5 wheat tiles (T=5) → sells 8. A chain of 5 wheat (v=2) also pays floor(5×2/2)=5 coins.
  • 1 bread = 3 flour + 1 egg = 15 wheat + 6 bird tiles → sells 125. Raw 3 flour = 24c, so the Bakery ≈ 5× multiplier — the first big economic inflection (Town 1).
  • 1 supplies = 5 flour = 25 wheat tiles → 1 expedition turn (Town 2). 1 mine trip (3 turns) = 3 supplies = 15 flour = 75 wheat tiles. That's the throttle; the sim will say if 75 is too steep (candidate: supplies = flour×3 = 45 tiles).
Concrete balance bugs to fix (grounded in the configs — see §15):
  • BUG bread sells 125 in ITEMS but 4 in MARKET_PRICES — a stale market row. Make market sell = item value (bread = 125).
  • BUG Miner worker reduces category "wood" (workers/data.ts) — should target a mine category (e.g. mine_stone).
  • BUG First hire ≈ inert. Reductions fold as amount × hired / maxCount with maxCount:10, so hire #1 = 0.1 tiles. Fix: lower maxCount to ~5, or floor per-hire so hire #1 = a clean −1.
  • CHANGE Trim FARM_TILE_POOL — today it's 10 categories from the first board (hay×3 + 9 singles). Stage-0 pool = hay×4, wheat×2; add a category as its tier unlocks.
  • CHANGE Drop block from early farm buildings (Bakery's 10 block) — it's a mine resource; Town 1 has no mine.
  • CHANGE Re-gate buildings from lv to tier/flag (§3).

8 · The long-term sink — capacity ladder

The Old Capital is the endgame sink (deferred with the meta). The mid-game sink — active from Town 2 (the first mine) — is a rising capacity ladder, a clean PC2-Castle analog that answers "what do I keep improving?"

11 · The deferred layer — net-new reward homes

The principle that unblocks the meta. "What's the reward for keepers/bosses, when global rewards are already strong?" is a supply problem: the power economy (workers, free turns, tools, tiers, capacity ladder) is saturated, so any new power reward is redundant or game-breaking. The fix is to add new demand, not more supply — build a net-new system the player wants to feed, and the meta rewards have a home that's impossible to get elsewhere. Don't run a parallel multiplier economy.
  • The "third pillar" slot (where PC2 puts its sea puzzle) is reserved for our own thing — most likely a collection / curation pillar. The Coexist/Drive-Out choice then becomes which collection you build: a living menagerie of spared spirits vs a trophy hall of bested ones. Bosses each drop a unique specimen/trophy. The reward is prestige & completion — non-breaking by construction.
  • Keepers return as the settlement capstone (surface only after town 1) → a qualitative board-rule fork + the Hearth-Token (pure progression).
  • Old Capital = the endgame restoration sink; Charter "reads your record" of choices.
  • Progression innovation (parked seeds): settlements that buff each other once both restored; a kingdom map that visibly reconnects; a "pick which ruin next" menu. Revisit after towns 1–3 play.
