How to read growth. Watch the roads: Outpost is one short main street; Hamlet extends it; Village opens a back lane behind it (the first new route); City knits in cross-connectors + a second back lane and cobbles the centre. Buildings line the streets at a setback, the forest is cleared along each new road, and the central plaza + farm board stay a fixed size (they never grow over the buildings).

Why redo it

The old town was a placeholder lattice; the first attempt fixed the look but drew a road straight from every building to the centre — so roads crossed other buildings, plots crowded, and the plaza grew over them. The fix (below) is the model real towns and city-builders use: lay the roads first, then place buildings fronting them.

Design principles

Roads first, buildings front the street. Never route a road to a building's centre. Lay a road, then place buildings beside it at a fixed setback. Roads always run between buildings. (Burgage-plot frontage; Parish & Müller; Manor Lords.)
Roads grow → new routes. Each rung adds a clear new piece of network: extend the main street, open a back lane, add cross-connectors. The road map itself tells the growth story. (Watabou village generator; ribbon development.)
Frontage spacing. Lots get a minimum street-frontage width, so buildings are evenly spaced and never crowd. (Procedural lot subdivision; medieval pole-measured plots.)
A bounded plaza buildings ring. The market square / plaza is a fixed open hub. Buildings front its perimeter; it never expands over them. Streets radiate from it. (Medieval market squares.)
Forest clears along the roads. The wilderness recedes wherever a new road and its frontage open up — so growth reads as the town pushing into the woods, route by route.
Additive & stable. Roads are immutable once laid; new growth only extends the network and appends lots. A placed building (and its street) never moves.

well → fountain → monument; a plot-sized tilled farm field.
